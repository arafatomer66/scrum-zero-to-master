# PSD — Professional Scrum Developer

*Scrum.org's most directly technical certification — self-study like PSM and PSPO, but the one credential in this guide where the material itself demands you already write code for a living.*

Professional Scrum Developer validates technical Scrum practice specifically from inside the Developers' accountability — not the framework's events and artifacts in the abstract, but the actual engineering habits (test-driven development, continuous integration, refactoring, managing technical debt) that make Scrum's empirical, iterative promise real rather than aspirational. Like PSM and PSPO, it's an open-book, self-paced assessment on Scrum.org's own platform with no mandatory course — a hands-on "Applying Professional Scrum for Software Development" course exists and is genuinely useful preparation, but it's optional, the same way PSM's and PSPO's companion courses are optional. (Scrum.org dropped a formal course requirement for PSD back in 2013 — if you've heard PSD described elsewhere as "the one that requires a class first," that's outdated.)

## Who It's For

PSD is for Developers — in the Scrum Guide's accountability sense, covering engineers, testers, and anyone doing hands-on build work on a Scrum Team — who want a credential that specifically validates technical Scrum practice, not just framework knowledge. It's aimed at people who already write code for a living and want to prove they can apply Scrum's empirical, iterative approach to the actual mechanics of building software: emergent design instead of big upfront architecture, continuous integration instead of long-lived branches, test-driven development instead of testing as an afterthought, and disciplined management of technical debt as an ongoing practice rather than a cleanup sprint that never comes.

It is not the right fit for someone in a Scrum Master or Product Owner accountability looking for a credential — PSM and PSPO exist for exactly those tracks, and PSD's content assumes you're doing the actual coding, not facilitating or ordering a backlog. See [Developers in The Scrum Team](../../02-scrum-framework/scrum-team.md) for the accountability PSD is built around before deciding it's your next certification.

## Format & Logistics

- **Format:** Self-study, open-book, timed online assessment on Scrum.org's own platform — the same delivery model as PSM I and PSPO I. No mandatory course; you can prepare entirely on your own and sit the assessment whenever you're ready. **Verify current question count, time limit, and passing score on scrum.org** — treat it as broadly in the same band as PSM I/PSPO I (roughly 80 questions, ~60 minutes, ~85% to pass) unless scrum.org's own page says otherwise when you check.
- **The optional course:** Scrum.org and its licensed trainers offer "Applying Professional Scrum for Software Development" (commonly a multi-day, hands-on class built around a real, evolving codebase and kata-style exercises in TDD, refactoring, and CI). It's genuinely strong preparation — arguably the single best way to build the muscle memory PSD tests — but it is not required to sit the assessment, and completing it doesn't automatically grant the certification either; you still take the assessment separately.
- **Cost:** The assessment fee itself sits in the same tier as PSM I/PSPO I. Cost only rises meaningfully if you choose to also take the optional live course, priced separately by the training provider — **verify current assessment and course pricing on scrum.org.**
- **Retake policy:** Standard Scrum.org retake terms apply if you don't pass. **Verify current retake terms on scrum.org.**
- **Prerequisites:** No formal experience requirement and no mandatory course, but come in already comfortable writing code — this assessment tests applying Scrum to technical practice, not learning to program.
- **Validity:** Does not expire, no renewal fee, consistent with every other Scrum.org credential in this guide.

## What It Actually Tests

PSD tests technical Scrum practice specifically from inside the Developers' accountability — how a team applies Scrum's empirical approach to the actual work of writing, testing, integrating, and evolving software, not the framework's events and artifacts in the abstract. Where PSM asks about facilitation and PSPO asks about value maximization, PSD asks: given Scrum's insistence that software gets built through short, empirical cycles rather than upfront design, what technical practices actually make that possible, and can you apply them, not just describe them.

Concretely, expect the assessment to cover:

- **Test-driven development** — writing a failing test first, the minimum code to pass it, then refactoring, as the concrete mechanism that makes emergent design safe. PSD treats TDD as a practiced skill, not a slide you memorize.
- **Continuous integration** — merging small, frequent changes into a shared mainline rather than accumulating long-lived branches, and understanding why CI is what keeps an Increment genuinely integrated and releasable every Sprint rather than integrated in a scramble at the end.
- **Refactoring as a continuous discipline** — improving a design's internal structure without changing its external behavior, done constantly as understanding grows, rather than treated as a separate "refactoring sprint" that competes with feature work and therefore never actually happens.
- **Emergent design** — designing for what's known now, kept cheap to change, instead of front-loading architecture decisions before any real feedback exists. This is the technical practice that makes Scrum's complex-work framing (see [Emergent Software Development](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/04-developing-and-delivering-products-professionally.md)) actually operational rather than theoretical.
- **Managing technical debt** — recognizing that a shortcut taken under Sprint pressure compounds like financial debt, and building the habits (a real Definition of Done, disciplined refactoring, honest inspection of code quality at the Sprint Review) that keep debt visible and managed instead of silently accumulating until it stalls a team a year later.
- **Applying Scrum specifically from the Developers' accountability** — self-managing how the work gets done, holding each other accountable as professionals, adapting the Sprint Backlog daily toward the Sprint Goal, and instilling quality by actually adhering to the team's Definition of Done rather than treating it as a checklist filled in after the fact.

For the conceptual grounding behind all of this — why complex work has to emerge rather than be designed upfront, why technical debt is a whole-team risk and not just an engineering inconvenience — [Developing and Delivering Products Professionally](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/04-developing-and-delivering-products-professionally.md) covers exactly this ground.

## Study Plan

1. **Read [Developing and Delivering Products Professionally](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/04-developing-and-delivering-products-professionally.md) first**, so the conceptual vocabulary — emergent design, managing technical risk, the Definition of Done as a transparency mechanism — is solid before you tie it to specific technical practices.
2. **Get genuinely hands-on with TDD, refactoring, and CI if you aren't already**, through a kata site, a book, or your own codebase. This is the material PSD actually tests — reading about TDD and having done it repeatedly under time pressure are different kinds of knowledge, and the assessment rewards the latter.
3. **Consider the optional "Applying Professional Scrum for Software Development" course** if you want structured, guided practice with a trainer and a cohort — not required, but a genuinely efficient way to build the muscle memory, especially if self-directed kata practice isn't sticking.
4. **Review [The Scrum Team](../../02-scrum-framework/scrum-team.md) and [The Product Backlog](../../02-scrum-framework/product-backlog.md) for accountability boundaries** — PSD assessment questions test whether you understand where the Developers' authority over "how" ends and the Product Owner's authority over "what" and "why" begins, the same boundary tested from the other side by PSPO.
5. **Practice explaining *why*, not just *how*.** PSD isn't a programming exam — it repeatedly asks why a technical practice matters to Scrum's empiricism, not just whether you can perform it. If you can do TDD but can't articulate why it makes emergent design safe, that gap is exactly what the assessment probes.

## Common Failure Points

- **Assuming strong coding skill alone is sufficient.** PSD's hardest trap for genuinely good developers is that being a strong engineer doesn't automatically mean you understand the *Scrum* framing of technical practice. A developer who's excellent at TDD but has never thought about why the Definition of Done exists as a transparency mechanism — not just a quality gate, but the thing that keeps a Sprint Review's inspection honest — will misread questions that hinge on that distinction.
- **Treating the Definition of Done as a checklist instead of a transparency mechanism.** This is worth naming specifically because it's the single most common conceptual gap: a DoD isn't there to make sure the code is good (though it does that too); it's there so that when the team calls something "Done," everyone inspecting it — the Product Owner, stakeholders, the team itself next Sprint — can trust that word means the same thing every time. A quietly watered-down DoD isn't just a quality problem, it's an empiricism problem, because every decision made from a "Done" increment that isn't really done inherits false confidence.
- **Skipping hands-on practice and trying to read your way to fluency.** Unlike PSM/PSPO, where re-reading reference material genuinely gets you most of the way there, PSD tests practiced skill. Someone who's only ever read about TDD, without doing it repeatedly, tends to underperform relative to how well they know the concepts on paper.
- **Forgetting the accountability boundary and answering like a Product Owner or Scrum Master.** PSD questions are angled from inside the Developers' accountability — self-managing the "how," instilling quality, adapting the plan daily. An answer that has a Developer unilaterally deciding what to build (a Product Owner call) or that hands authority over technical practice to someone outside the Developers (a Scrum Master or manager overstepping) is wrong regardless of how reasonable it sounds.

PSD's real differentiator from the rest of Scrum.org's catalog isn't its format — it's self-study like everything else here — it's the subject matter: the only credential in this guide that tests hands-on technical practice rather than facilitation, product, or leadership skill.
