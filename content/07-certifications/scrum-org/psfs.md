# PSFS — Professional Scrum Facilitation Skills

*Scrum.org's specialty credential for facilitation technique itself — divergent and convergent thinking, the neutral facilitation stance, handling group dynamics and conflict, and designing effective sessions rather than just running the standard Scrum events on autopilot.*

Professional Scrum Facilitation Skills tests the deepest, most deliberately isolated slice of the facilitation skillset among every credential this guide covers. Plenty of Scrum Masters "run" the Daily Scrum, Sprint Planning, and the Sprint Retrospective in the sense that they show up, keep time, and ask the standard questions — and never actually facilitate any of them, in the sense of designing the session's structure, managing group dynamics, and staying neutral on content while actively shaping process. PSFS exists specifically to separate those two things and certify the second one. It's less about knowing Scrum's event agendas — which PSFS assumes — and almost entirely about the craft of running any group session well, Scrum event or not.

## Who It's For

PSFS is aimed squarely at Scrum Masters and Agile Coaches who treat facilitation as a professional skill worth deliberately developing, not something that happens automatically once you've sat in enough meetings. It's equally relevant to anyone outside a formal Scrum Master title who regularly runs group sessions professionally — a lead facilitating cross-functional workshops, a coach running alignment sessions across teams, anyone whose job increasingly looks like "the person who makes group decision-making actually work" rather than "the person with the meeting invite."

It's not the right starting point if you haven't yet run real Scrum events under real conditions — PSFS questions assume you've experienced a dominant voice derailing a Retrospective, or silence killing a brainstorm, and are testing whether you know the deliberate countermeasure, not whether you've encountered the problem at all. If you're newer to the role, get real reps in first via [The Scrum Master Role](../../04-scrum-master-role/) before this exam.

## Format & Logistics

PSFS follows Scrum.org's standard specialist-exam structure: open-book, timed, taken independently through Scrum.org's own platform, no proctor, no mandatory course as a prerequisite. Multiple choice, multiple answer, and true/false questions, historically around 20 questions in roughly 30 minutes, with a passing bar in the same high-80s-percent range Scrum.org uses across its assessments. Pricing sits in a similar tier to Scrum.org's other specialist credentials — a single flat exam fee, no bundled course. **Verify the current question count, time limit, passing score, and price on scrum.org before registering**, since these details are periodically adjusted and this guide's numbers are meant as a planning ballpark, not a guarantee. Like every Scrum.org credential, PSFS does not expire and carries no ongoing renewal fee.

## What It Actually Tests

PSFS is the exam that most directly maps to a single, cohesive skill area rather than testing knowledge spread across a whole role. Expect scenario-heavy questions on:

- **Divergent versus convergent thinking as two distinct, deliberately separated phases** — recognizing when a session is trying to generate options and evaluate them simultaneously (a reliable way to produce shallow, prematurely-narrowed outcomes), and knowing the concrete techniques that force the separation: structured idea generation before any critique, then a distinct convergence phase using something like affinity grouping or dot-voting.
- **The neutral facilitation stance** — staying actively neutral on session *content* while actively shaping session *process*, including recognizing when a facilitator has crossed from neutral process guidance into steering the group toward their own preferred answer, even unintentionally through phrasing or body language.
- **Handling group dynamics and conflict inside a live session** — managing a dominant voice without publicly embarrassing them, drawing out silence without rushing to fill it yourself, recognizing when unresolved tension between participants (not just quietness or one loud voice) is the actual root cause of a stuck conversation.
- **Designing a session for its actual purpose**, not defaulting to the same format every time — choosing an appropriate structure and technique set based on whether the goal is generating ideas, making a decision, resolving a disagreement, or building shared understanding, and recognizing when a session's stated goal and its actual structure don't match.
- **Scrum event design specifically** — applying all of the above inside Sprint Planning, the Daily Scrum, the Sprint Review, and the Sprint Retrospective, since these are the sessions a Scrum Master facilitates most often and the exam expects you to translate general facilitation technique into these specific containers.

## Study Plan

1. **Work through [Facilitation Techniques](../../04-scrum-master-role/facilitation-techniques.md) in this guide.** This is the core prep material for PSFS in this repo, and the closest 1:1 competency match to any certification covered here — it walks through diverge-before-converge, dot-voting, silent brainstorming, timeboxing discipline, the parking lot, staying neutral while shaping process, handling a dominant voice, and handling silence, all at the level of concrete, nameable technique rather than general advice.
2. **Read [Conflict Resolution](../../04-scrum-master-role/conflict-resolution.md)** for the specific case where a stuck facilitation is actually a symptom of unresolved interpersonal tension rather than a pure group-dynamics problem — PSFS scenario questions frequently hinge on telling these two situations apart and applying the right response to each.
3. **Read [Coaching vs. Mentoring vs. Teaching](../../04-scrum-master-role/coaching-vs-mentoring-vs-teaching.md)** for the adjacent skill of addressing a recurring facilitation problem (like a chronically dominant voice) outside the room, privately, as a coaching conversation rather than a public correction — a distinction PSFS expects you to hold onto.
4. **Practice designing session structures for purposes other than the standard Scrum events.** PSFS doesn't limit itself to Scrum ceremonies — take a real, messy group decision you need to make (at work or otherwise) and deliberately design a divergent-then-convergent structure for it, complete with timeboxes and a parking lot, rather than defaulting to an open discussion.
5. **Actually facilitate something using silent brainstorming and dot-voting before the exam**, if you haven't already. The techniques read as obvious on the page and feel different the first time you run them live — questions that test "what would you do when the room goes quiet" land very differently once you've sat through that silence yourself and resisted the urge to fill it.

## Common Failure Points

- **Conflating facilitation with just running the standard Scrum events on schedule.** Showing up, following the standard agenda, and keeping time is event management, not facilitation. PSFS tests the deliberate craft underneath that — session design, neutral stance, group-dynamics handling — and an answer that treats "we held the Retrospective" as equivalent to "we facilitated the Retrospective well" is missing the entire point of the exam.
- **Missing when neutrality has actually been broken.** People correctly know "stay neutral" as a rule but miss subtler violations — leading questions, selectively acknowledging ideas that match their own preference, or body language that signals approval or disapproval. PSFS scenario questions are often built around exactly this kind of subtle drift, not obvious cases of a facilitator openly pushing an agenda.
- **Treating divergence and convergence as the same activity.** An answer that has the group generating and evaluating ideas simultaneously, even if it sounds efficient, is the anti-pattern PSFS is testing against — premature convergence is one of the most consistently tested failure modes on this exam.
- **Responding to a dominant voice or persistent silence with a generic "encourage participation" answer** instead of a concrete technique. PSFS rewards specific, nameable moves — round-robin, written input first, a private coaching conversation for a repeat pattern — over vague good intentions.
- **Missing the difference between a group-dynamics problem and an unresolved-conflict problem.** Applying a pure facilitation technique (like dot-voting) to a situation that's actually about interpersonal tension between two participants won't resolve it, and PSFS expects you to recognize which tool the situation actually calls for.
