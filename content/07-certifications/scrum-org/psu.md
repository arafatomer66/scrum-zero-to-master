# PSU — Professional Scrum with User Experience

*Scrum.org's specialty credential for closing the gap between design and development — integrating UX practice inside the Sprint instead of running it as a separate phase ahead of "real" development.*

Professional Scrum with User Experience tests whether you understand how design work fits inside Scrum's cadence rather than in front of it. The failure mode PSU exists to correct is extremely common and rarely named honestly: a team calls itself agile, runs Sprints, holds all the events — and still has a UX designer working a Sprint or two ahead of the Developers, handing off finished mockups like a waterfall stage-gate with a Scrum-shaped wrapper around it. That arrangement isn't UX integrated into Scrum; it's UX bolted onto the outside of Scrum, and it reproduces most of the coordination problems Scrum exists to solve — late discovery of usability problems, designers and Developers who rarely talk mid-Sprint, and a Definition of Done that only checks whether code works, never whether anyone can actually use it.

## Who It's For

PSU is for designers and UX practitioners working on Scrum Teams who want a credential proving they understand Scrum well enough to integrate into it, not just tolerate it. It's equally relevant for Scrum Masters, Product Owners, and Developers who want to understand what good UX integration actually looks like in practice — because the Scrum Master is often the person who has to notice and fix a "design phase ahead of the Sprint" anti-pattern, and the Product Owner is the one writing Product Backlog items that either do or don't carry real usability criteria.

It assumes solid Scrum fundamentals already in place — PSU tests the UX-specific overlay, not the base framework. If your Scrum knowledge is shaky, [PSM I](psm-i.md) or a pass through [section 02, The Scrum Framework](../../02-scrum-framework/) is the right prerequisite, not this exam.

## Format & Logistics

PSU follows Scrum.org's standard specialist-exam format: open-book, timed, delivered on Scrum.org's own platform with no proctor and no mandatory training course required beforehand. Multiple choice, multiple answer, and true/false questions, historically in the range of 60 questions and 60 minutes, with a passing bar around 85%, in line with Scrum.org's other single-tier assessments. Pricing sits in a similar tier to PSM I and PSK — a single flat fee per attempt, no course purchase required. **Verify the current question count, time limit, passing score, and price directly on scrum.org before registering**, since none of these figures are fixed permanently and this guide won't always catch a change the same day it happens. Like every Scrum.org credential, PSU doesn't expire and has no renewal requirement once earned.

## What It Actually Tests

PSU tests your ability to reason about where UX work sits relative to the Sprint boundary, how design decisions get validated, and how a Scrum Team collaborates on design rather than handing it off. Broadly, expect scenario questions probing: whether a described workflow keeps design and development in the same Sprint or artificially separates them; whether usability validation happens through real collaborative techniques or gets skipped in favor of a designer's unilateral judgment; and whether a team's Definition of Done accounts for usability at all, or only for code correctness.

## Study Plan

Because this repo doesn't yet have a dedicated UX-competency section the way it has one for flow or facilitation, the grounding for this exam lives here rather than pointing you elsewhere first.

**The core problem PSU is built around: the design-development gap.** In a traditional handoff model, UX research and design happen in a phase ahead of the Sprint — sometimes called "Sprint Zero" or a "design sprint" running structurally separate from delivery. By the time Developers start building, the design is treated as fixed, feedback from real usage arrives too late to act on cheaply, and the Sprint's own inspect-and-adapt cycle never actually touches the design itself, only the code implementing it. This is a waterfall stage-gate wearing Scrum's vocabulary. PSU's whole premise is that this gap is avoidable, and that avoiding it is a skill, not a luck of team culture.

**The fix: continuous, in-Sprint UX work, not a separate discovery phase before delivery.** Rather than treating "when do we design" as a phase question, treat it as a continuous activity running alongside delivery — UX work on the next slice of the product happens in loose parallel with Developers building the current slice, with tight, frequent touchpoints between the two rather than a single handoff. A designer isn't a Sprint ahead delivering finished specs; they're inside the same Sprint, refining and validating design for upcoming Product Backlog items while Developers build items that were already validated. This only works if the designer and Developers are talking daily, not just at Sprint Planning and Sprint Review — the [Sprint](../../02-scrum-framework/sprint.md) is a single container for all of this work, not a boundary UX work happens outside of.

**Collaborative design techniques replace solo design-then-present.** PSU expects familiarity with lightweight, team-involving design methods rather than a lone designer producing polished mockups in isolation: structured design workshops where multiple team members sketch competing solutions independently before converging (a technique often called a Design Studio), paper prototyping that's cheap enough to throw away after a single round of feedback, card sorting and quick guerrilla usability tests with a handful of real users rather than a formal, weeks-long research study. The throughline is speed and involvement — validate small design decisions cheaply and often, with the whole team present for at least some of it, instead of validating one big design decision expensively and alone.

**Usability belongs in the Definition of Done, not as a separate quality track.** A Product Backlog item that's functionally complete but genuinely unusable isn't Done under a Scrum Team that's actually integrated UX — the same way an item that skipped code review isn't Done regardless of whether it "works." See [Definition of Done](../../02-scrum-framework/definition-of-done.md) for how a team's quality bar is meant to evolve as it learns what it's been missing; a team that adds "usability-tested with at least one real user" to its Definition of Done after repeatedly shipping features nobody could figure out how to use is doing exactly what that evolution is meant to look like. PSU questions frequently probe whether a described scenario's Definition of Done actually accounts for usability or only checks technical correctness.

**Acceptance criteria should describe user outcomes, not just functional behavior.** A Product Backlog item written as "user can reset their password" with acceptance criteria limited to "the reset email sends and the new password saves" passes a narrow functional test while saying nothing about whether the actual flow is usable. PSU expects you to recognize when acceptance criteria need a usability dimension — task completion, error recovery, discoverability — alongside the functional one, and to know that writing that dimension in is a Product Owner and UX collaboration, not an afterthought bolted on by a separate QA pass.

## Common Failure Points

- **Answering as if UX work happens in a phase before the Sprint.** Any answer describing design as "finished before development starts" or "a separate track that feeds into Sprints" reflects the exact anti-pattern PSU is testing against, even if it sounds like normal industry practice.
- **Treating usability testing as optional or as something a lone designer decides alone.** PSU expects collaborative validation involving the team and real users, not one person's unchallenged judgment presented as settled.
- **Ignoring the Definition of Done as a lever for usability.** People who've only studied the base Scrum Guide sometimes forget the Definition of Done is a live, team-owned bar that can and should include non-functional criteria like usability — not just "compiles and passes tests."
- **Confusing "involving a UX practitioner" with "integrating UX practice."** Having a designer attend the Daily Scrum doesn't by itself close the design-development gap if their actual work still runs a Sprint ahead on a separate track — PSU tests the underlying integration, not just attendance.
- **Assuming PSU is a general design or UX-research certification.** It isn't. It's specifically about UX practice *inside Scrum's structure* — general design skill without Scrum fluency underperforms on this exam just as badly as Scrum fluency without design skill does.
