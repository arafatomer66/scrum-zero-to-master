# A Manager Assigns Tasks to Individuals

*The Developers' functional manager has started handing out work directly, bypassing the Sprint Backlog entirely — and the team has quietly started letting him.*

## The Situation

Team Cormorant builds internal analytics tooling at a mid-size logistics firm. The six Developers report, on the org chart, to an engineering manager named Petros, who is not on the Scrum Team and doesn't attend Sprint Planning. Two weeks ago, Petros messaged a developer, Yuki, directly: "Can you pick up the warehouse-dashboard export bug today? Ops is escalating." Yuki did — it wasn't on the Sprint Board, it wasn't discussed at Planning, and it displaced two hours she'd have spent on a Sprint Backlog item.

It's happened four more times since, to three different developers, always the same pattern: a direct message from Petros, framed as urgent, picked up without question because he's the person who writes their performance reviews. The Scrum Master, Bilal, notices the Sprint Board increasingly doesn't reflect what people are actually working on day to day — there's a growing gap between the board and reality, and the Developers have started treating the board as somewhat aspirational, updating it after the fact rather than using it to decide what to do next.

## Why This Happens

This pattern is one of the most common anti-patterns in organizations transitioning to Scrum from a traditional functional-management structure, because the reporting line and the accountability structure haven't actually changed even though the ceremony has:

- **Petros still evaluates performance, approves raises, and can make Yuki's work life materially harder or easier** — none of that authority moved when the org adopted Scrum. Developers will rationally prioritize the person who controls their career over a Sprint Backlog that, from their seat, has no comparable enforcement mechanism.
- **"Ops is escalating" framing makes every request feel individually reasonable** — nobody wants to be the developer who told their manager "no, that's not on my board" when a customer-facing system is on fire, even if the actual urgency doesn't hold up to scrutiny.
- **Petros likely doesn't see this as bypassing anything** — from a traditional management mindset, assigning work to the people who report to him is simply what managers do; he may not have a clear model of what changed about his role when the team adopted Scrum, because nobody explicitly told him.
- **Self-management erodes quietly, not dramatically.** No single instance looks like a crisis — it's one bug, once. But five instances across three developers in two weeks means the team's actual working reality is now shaped by a management chain the Scrum framework explicitly moved that authority away from, and the Sprint Backlog has stopped being a reliable plan.

## The Scrum Master's Approach

1. **Talk to the Developers first, not Petros, to understand the real pattern.** Bilal asks Yuki and the two others directly: how many times has this happened, how urgent were the requests really, and how did it feel to say yes versus how it would have felt to say no. This grounds Bilal's next conversation in specifics rather than a general impression, and surfaces whether the team feels able to push back at all — which is itself important information.
2. **Talk to Petros directly, assuming good intent, and make the actual cost visible.** Bilal frames it in terms Petros will recognize: "In the last two weeks, five items got picked up outside the Sprint Backlog based on direct requests to individuals — that's real capacity that didn't go toward what the team committed to at Planning, and the Sprint Board no longer reflects what's actually being worked. I want to find a way to get you what you need without that side effect." This is a [working with difficult stakeholders](../04-scrum-master-role/working-with-difficult-stakeholders.md) conversation even though Petros isn't a classic external stakeholder — he's adjacent to the team with real influence over it, which is its own category worth naming.
3. **Give Petros a real channel instead of just closing off the informal one.** Bilal proposes that urgent operational requests go to the Product Owner (or, for genuine emergencies, to Bilal directly) so they can be weighed against what's already committed and, if truly urgent, brought to the team transparently rather than routed around them. An unmet need doesn't disappear just because the informal channel that was meeting it gets shut down — it has to be replaced with a legitimate one or it'll route around the fix.
4. **Coach the Developers on how to redirect the next request without it feeling like defiance.** Bilal role-plays a response with Yuki: "That sounds urgent — let me flag it to [Product Owner] so we can see what it displaces, and I'll circle back within the hour." This protects the team's self-management without putting an individual developer in the position of unilaterally refusing their manager, which is an unfair burden to place on any one person. This is a [shielding vs. enabling](../04-scrum-master-role/shielding-vs-enabling.md) judgment — Bilal is shielding the team's ability to self-manage, not shielding them from operational reality.
5. **Restore the Sprint Board as the actual source of truth**, which requires the previous four steps to hold, not just a announcement. Bilal checks in daily for the next two Sprints specifically on whether anything off-board is happening, rather than assuming one conversation with Petros fixed a five-instance pattern permanently.
6. **Name this explicitly as an anti-pattern if it continues after direct coaching**, and escalate appropriately — to whoever Petros reports to, or to whoever owns the org's Scrum adoption — because a functional manager persistently overriding a Sprint Backlog isn't a one-off misunderstanding at that point, it's a structural conflict between the org chart and the operating model that needs resolving above the team level. See [anti-patterns and Scrum smells](../04-scrum-master-role/anti-patterns-and-scrum-smells.md).

## Likely Outcome

Petros, once the cost is made concrete rather than abstract, is receptive — he genuinely didn't think of it as undermining anything, and agrees to route requests through the Product Owner going forward. The first test comes ten days later: a similarly "urgent" request, and Petros actually does route it through the PO rather than DMing a developer directly, which the team notices and takes as a real signal, not just a promise. One developer, months later, still occasionally gets a direct ping for something small and quietly redirects it herself without needing Bilal — a sign the coaching, not just the one conversation with Petros, is what actually stuck.

## Certification Exam Angle

A standard PSM I framing: *"A manager outside the Scrum Team directly assigns work to individual Developers, bypassing the Sprint Backlog. What should the Scrum Master do?"* The trap answer is often "tell the Developers to refuse any work not on the Sprint Backlog" — this places an unreasonable burden on individual contributors to police their own manager, ignores the real power asymmetry involved, and is unlikely to actually work, since compliance under an implicit threat to career standing doesn't respond well to a blanket instruction.

A second trap answer: "escalate immediately to Petros's manager" — skipping a direct, good-faith conversation and going straight to escalation is disproportionate for a first occurrence and tends to create an adversarial dynamic that makes future coaching harder, not easier. The correct-mindset answer has the Scrum Master engage the manager directly, framing the issue in terms of impact rather than accusation, establishing a legitimate channel for genuinely urgent requests, and coaching the Developers on how to redirect future requests — because protecting the team's self-management is a core Scrum Master accountability, and it's achieved primarily through facilitation and coaching across the whole system (including people outside the Scrum Team), not through unilateral rules imposed on the Developers alone.
