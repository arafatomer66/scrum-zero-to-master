# Coaching a Product Owner Who Doesn't Trust Their Own Authority

*A newly promoted Product Owner is technically sharp but keeps deferring every prioritization call to the Developers, because she isn't sure she's actually allowed to just decide.*

## The Situation

Ayesha Malik was promoted six weeks ago from senior QA engineer to Product Owner for Team Ember at Solstice Fintech, replacing a Product Owner who left the company. She's technically excellent and well-liked, but entirely new to product ownership. The backlog has started accumulating items like "Improve the reconciliation report" — no specifics on what "improve" actually means, no stated outcome.

At refinement, when a developer asks whether the export feature or multi-currency support should come first, Ayesha says: "What do you all think makes more sense?" This is the third Sprint running she's deferred an ordering call to the team. Scrum Master Julian Voss later sees a Slack message Ayesha accidentally sent to a channel he's in, addressed to a colleague: "I don't actually know if I'm supposed to just tell them what to build or if that's too directive — nobody really explained where the line is." In standup, developer Kwabena Owusu jokes, "Should we just make Ayesha's decisions by poll?" People laugh. Julian notices it isn't really a joke.

## Why This Happens

- **Promoting someone into Product Ownership from a technically adjacent role — QA, development, business analysis — without explicit training or explicit authority-granting is a common and specific failure mode.** Ayesha has the title but not yet the confidence, or the organizational cover, to use it.
- **"Servant leadership" and "self-managing teams," absorbed secondhand rather than properly taught, sometimes get misread by new Product Owners as "don't tell the team what to do."** That principle applies to how work gets done, which belongs to the Developers — not to backlog ordering, which remains squarely the Product Owner's call.
- **Vague acceptance criteria is often a confidence problem wearing a skill problem's clothes.** Ayesha can write a precise QA test case without hesitation; she hasn't yet made the mental shift that specifying a desired outcome, not an implementation, is a skill she already half has from that background.
- **Nobody has told Ayesha explicitly what's actually hers to decide**, so she's guessing at the boundary in both directions at once — writing loose criteria and avoiding ordering decisions are two symptoms of the same underlying uncertainty.

## The Scrum Master's Approach

1. **Name what's being observed directly and kindly, in a 1:1.** Julian opens with recognition, not criticism: "I've noticed you've asked the team to make the last three ordering calls, and the reconciliation-report item was pretty open-ended. I don't think that's a skill gap — I think nobody's told you clearly what's actually yours to decide."
2. **Make the accountability boundary explicit, grounded in the Scrum Guide rather than Julian's opinion.** Ordering the [Product Backlog](../02-scrum-framework/product-backlog.md) is the Product Owner's sole call; how the team builds it is theirs; specifying what "done" and "valuable" mean for an item is squarely product's job, even if Developers help refine the detail — see [Managing Products with Agility](../03-professional-scrum-competencies/03-managing-products-with-agility.md) for the fuller model.
3. **Teach using a real example already in front of her, not an abstraction.** Julian takes the actual "improve the reconciliation report" item and coaches Ayesha through rewriting it live — what specific user complaint is driving this, what does success concretely look like, what's explicitly out of scope — turning an abstract skill into a worked example she can repeat. This is deliberately a teaching move rather than pure coaching or mentoring; see [coaching vs. mentoring vs. teaching](../04-scrum-master-role/coaching-vs-mentoring-vs-teaching.md) for why Julian chooses that mode here specifically.
4. **Give her a decision-making tool to lean on until judgment becomes automatic.** Julian introduces a simple value-versus-effort framework for the export-vs-multi-currency call specifically, so the next ordering decision has real scaffolding instead of asking her to trust an instinct she hasn't built yet.
5. **Explicitly authorize her to be directive where it's actually her job to be.** Julian tells her plainly that answering "the export feature, because it unblocks Q3 revenue from two named clients" at refinement isn't being controlling — it's doing the job. Being collaborative is about how she reaches and explains a decision, not about avoiding making one.
6. **Address the team's side of the dynamic too, not only Ayesha's.** Julian has a quiet word with Kwabena about the "decide by poll" comment — not to shut him down, but to note that the pattern is visible to the team and adds pressure in exactly the wrong direction — and asks the team to actively ask her "what's the goal here" rather than simply accepting deferred calls, reinforcing the right behavior from both sides at once.
7. **Check in after her next two or three ordering decisions and refinement sessions, closing the loop rather than assuming one conversation fixes it.** Julian watches for sharper acceptance criteria on new items and whether she owns the next ordering call without deflecting, adjusting how much teaching versus autonomy to offer based on what he actually sees.

## Likely Outcome

The rewritten reconciliation-report item becomes Ayesha's working template for acceptance criteria over the next month, and the difference is visible within two Sprints. The export-versus-multi-currency call becomes the first ordering decision she makes outright at refinement, citing the Q3 revenue reasoning Julian helped her surface — and Kwabena's later comment is "okay, that's actually useful, wish she'd done that three Sprints ago." She isn't fully confident yet; Julian still catches her hedging on a harder trade-off in Sprint 45. But the trajectory is clearly upward, and critically, the team has stopped quietly absorbing the Product Owner's decisions itself — which was the more dangerous long-term drift underneath the specific symptoms.

## Certification Exam Angle

A typical framing: *"A newly appointed Product Owner consistently defers prioritization decisions to the Development Team and writes vague acceptance criteria. What is the Scrum Master's most appropriate action?"* Trap answers include having the Scrum Master temporarily make backlog-ordering decisions until the Product Owner gains experience (the Scrum Master has no accountability for the backlog, and this just relocates the same abdication problem seen with an experienced Product Owner who won't decide — compare [scenario 02](02-product-owner-wont-prioritize.md)), telling the Development Team to keep making the calls since they seem comfortable doing it and it's faster (rewards and entrenches exactly the wrong long-term pattern, and the team is visibly uneasy about it, not actually comfortable), or reporting the Product Owner's performance to their manager as underperforming (skips straight to escalation on what is clearly a coachable confidence-and-clarity gap, not a competence or effort failure).

The correct-mindset answer recognizes this as a coaching and teaching opportunity squarely within the Scrum Master's accountability to the Product Owner, clarifies the actual boundary of Product Owner authority against the Scrum Guide rather than assumption, and uses concrete, real backlog examples to build the new Product Owner's confidence and skill — rather than either doing the job for them or reporting them for not yet having it. This connects to [scenario 14](14-po-not-available-for-the-team.md), a different Product Owner failure mode caused by capacity rather than confidence, and to [scenario 01](01-team-missing-every-sprint-goal.md) for the same underlying principle of coaching a role back to its actual accountability rather than absorbing it.
