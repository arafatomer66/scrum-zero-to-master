# "We Do Scrum Here" — When the Titles Changed and Nothing Else Did

*A newly certified Scrum Master joins a team where every ceremony is on the calendar, and discovers that renaming project managers doesn't mean anything about how decisions actually get made has changed.*

## The Situation

Maya Fenwick is six weeks into her first real Scrum Master role, supporting Team Cormorant at Haldane Systems, an insurance-underwriting SaaS vendor. She studied for and passed PSM I on her own time before taking the role. On paper, Haldane looks like a company that gets it: eighteen months ago it ran an "Agile Transformation," and every project manager in engineering went through a one-day certification-prep workshop and came out the other side with a new title — Scrum Master.

Team Cormorant runs Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, and Retrospective every two weeks like clockwork. But six weeks in, Maya has noticed what the calendar doesn't show. The Product Owner, Elena Kowalski, can't actually reorder the backlog: "I can't reprioritize, the roadmap's locked till November — corporate already presented it to the board," she tells Maya when asked why a clearly higher-value item is sitting behind three lower-value ones. A resource-allocation spreadsheet, maintained monthly by the PMO, has senior developer Raj Deshmukh split 60% Cormorant, 25% a project called Helios, 15% general support — and when Helios needs him mid-Sprint, he goes, Sprint Goal or no Sprint Goal. In Maya's own 1:1, her manager tells her, not unkindly: "Just keep the board updated and make sure the burn-down looks healthy for the steering committee deck." Sprint Review is seven people in a room demoing to each other; no stakeholder outside the PMO has attended in months.

## Why This Happens

This is the textbook shape of what Scrum.org calls "ScrumBut" — Scrum, but with the uncomfortable parts quietly removed — and it happens for reasons that are structural, not personal:

- **Renaming a title is cheap; redistributing decision authority is expensive.** A one-day workshop changes what's printed on a business card. It cannot, by itself, move backlog-ordering authority out of a steering committee's hands and into a Product Owner's.
- **The incentive structures that predate the transformation survive it.** Utilization percentages, annual roadmap sign-off, resourcing matrices — built for a project-management model, never dismantled — keep producing project-management behavior underneath a Scrum vocabulary.
- **Sprints get absorbed as a reporting cadence inside a fixed-scope plan**, rather than functioning as the actual unit of inspection and adaptation — functionally Waterfall with more standups.
- **The people closest to it often can't see it as ScrumBut**, because nobody's ever shown them the alternative — Maya's manager isn't being cynical when he asks for a healthy-looking burndown, he genuinely believes that's what "doing well" means here.

## The Scrum Master's Approach

1. **Diagnose decision rights before naming anything.** Maya spends part of her first real diagnostic pass mapping who actually decides backlog order, who actually allocates people, and how those map (or don't) to the accountabilities in the [Product Backlog](../02-scrum-framework/product-backlog.md) and the Scrum Team roles — quietly, as data-gathering, not as an accusation in search of a target.
2. **Quantify one concrete cost instead of arguing theory.** When Raj gets pulled to Helios mid-Sprint and the team misses its Sprint Goal for the second time that quarter, Maya documents it plainly: which goal, which item, how many points, how many days lost to the context switch.
3. **Bring it into the room as the team's own data, not her lecture.** At the next retrospective, she puts the pattern on the board — two of the last three Sprint Goals missed, both traceable to unplanned resourcing pulls — and lets the team name the impact themselves rather than being told what to think about it.
4. **Coach Elena on what Product Owner authority is actually supposed to include.** In a 1:1, Maya asks directly: "What's stopping you from moving this item up?" Elena's answer — the locked annual roadmap — surfaces the real constraint sitting one layer above the team, which Maya can't fix by coaching Elena harder.
5. **Reframe the finding upward as a business-cost conversation, not a compliance complaint.** Maya writes a short, factual one-pager for her manager's manager: not "we're not really doing Scrum," but "this specific resourcing pattern cost the team two Sprint Goals and roughly nine developer-days last quarter — here's the data."
6. **Propose one small, reversible experiment instead of demanding an overhaul.** She asks for one Sprint where Raj's allocation to Cormorant is fully protected, and invites a PMO steering committee member to that Sprint's Review to see the difference directly, rather than asking anyone to sign off on dismantling the resourcing matrix wholesale.
7. **Keep her own accountability honest regardless of the outcome.** Maya declines to dress up the burndown for the deck, says plainly in status updates why a number looks the way it looks, and treats naming this as [ScrumBut](../04-scrum-master-role/anti-patterns-and-scrum-smells.md) as part of the job description — while staying honest with herself that if nothing moves after real, sustained effort, that's information about whether this is a role she can keep doing in good conscience.

## Likely Outcome

Nothing about Haldane's operating model flips overnight — the annual roadmap doesn't get unlocked, and the resourcing matrix survives the quarter. But the one protected Sprint happens, the steering committee member who attends that Review sees the team demo something that actually reflects stakeholder-relevant progress for the first time, and gives real feedback instead of a nod. Elena starts pushing back, selectively, on resourcing pulls for items she's flagged as goal-critical. The pattern is named now, with data attached to it, which is different from where Maya started: six weeks ago this was invisible water everyone in the building was swimming in without noticing.

## Certification Exam Angle

A PSM II-style question built on this scenario often reads: *"Organizational leadership asks the Scrum Master to keep the Sprint burndown chart looking favorable for a steering committee report, in an organization where project managers were recently retitled Scrum Masters with no other change. What should the Scrum Master do?"* The tempting-but-wrong answers cluster around two extremes: quietly comply and make the chart look good since the roadmap is out of the Scrum Master's control (this abandons transparency, one of the five Scrum values, for organizational comfort), or refuse outright and escalate to HR immediately (skips every step of coaching and evidence-building that might actually move the organization, and reads as adversarial rather than as service).

The correct-mindset answer is to keep the data honest, make the dysfunction visible with concrete evidence rather than opinion, and work through influence and coaching — with the Product Owner, the team, and upward through the management chain — since a Scrum Master has no formal authority to unilaterally change an organization's structure. The exam is testing whether you understand ScrumBut as a common, survivable starting condition whose fix is patient, evidence-driven organizational coaching — see [the four Scrum Master service areas](../04-scrum-master-role/the-4-sm-service-areas.md) — not silent compliance and not a dramatic confrontation either.
