# When the Sprint Goal Becomes Obsolete Mid-Sprint

*A client's requirement change guts the Sprint Goal on day four — whether to invoke Sprint cancellation, how to adapt inside the Sprint instead, and what to do when this keeps happening with the same client.*

## The Situation

Team Compass builds invoicing software at Ridgeline Payments. Scrum Master Farah Nasser, Product Owner Devon Ilic. Sprint 22's goal: "Merchants can set up recurring invoices with configurable retry logic for failed payments." On day four, a large client — roughly 30% of the company's ARR — tells the account team that a compliance requirement has completely changed the failed-payment flow: it now has to route through a new third-party service instead of the one the team has been building against for three days.

Devon comes to Farah visibly rattled: "Everything we've built the last three days is either wrong or has to be reworked against an API we don't even have documented access to yet. I don't know if we should keep going or stop." The concrete state of the Sprint: two of five planned items are already merged and shippable regardless of the change. The other three are now either irrelevant or blocked on documentation for the new API, which the client's engineering contact estimates at "a few days, maybe next week."

## Why This Happens

- **Sprint Goals can become genuinely obsolete for reasons entirely outside the team's control.** This isn't sloppy planning — it's a real external requirement change — and Scrum's actual tool for this, Sprint cancellation, is rare enough in practice that many teams have never used it and default instead to grinding through confusion.
- **The Scrum Guide gives sole authority to cancel a Sprint to the Product Owner**, a fact many teams — and some Product Owners themselves, under pressure — forget or never fully internalized, defaulting instead to either silently abandoning the goal without formally addressing it, or stubbornly grinding toward a goal everyone already knows is dead.
- **Cancellation feels like reputational failure to a Product Owner worried about optics with leadership**, which biases toward avoiding it even when it's the right call — or, less commonly, toward overusing it when simply adapting scope inside the Sprint would have worked fine.
- **A single instance is normal; a repeated pattern points upstream.** If this keeps happening with the same client or source, a Sprint-level response alone can't fix what's actually a requirement-stability problem sitting one layer above the team.

## The Scrum Master's Approach

1. **Establish exactly what's dead versus salvageable, fast.** Farah facilitates a short working session — not a full re-plan — mapping the five original items against the new reality: two already merged and shippable regardless, one now genuinely pointless, two blocked on the undocumented API.
2. **Apply the Scrum Guide's actual bar for cancellation, not a gut feeling.** A [Sprint](../02-scrum-framework/sprint.md) Goal is cancelled when it becomes obsolete — not merely when scope needs adjusting. Here, roughly 40% of the goal's value, the two merged items plus usable groundwork, is still real, which leans toward adapting within the Sprint rather than cancelling it outright.
3. **Be explicit about whose decision this is, and support it cleanly.** Farah tells Devon directly that cancelling a Sprint is the Product Owner's decision alone; her job is to make sure he has the information to decide well and to facilitate the mechanics — a re-planning session, clear communication to the team — whichever way he decides, not to make the call herself.
4. **Adapt rather than cancel, in this case.** Farah facilitates rewriting the Sprint Goal that same day to something honest and still achievable: "Merchants can set up recurring invoices; failed-payment retry logic is scoped and ready to build the moment the new API is documented" — turning three days of now-obsolete work into a goal the team can still believe in, rather than limping toward one everyone already knows is false.
5. **Make the change transparent to stakeholders immediately, not at Sprint Review.** Devon sends a short note that same day to the account team and relevant stakeholders explaining what changed and why, rather than letting Sprint Review be the first time anyone outside the team hears about it.
6. **Log the rework cost honestly instead of letting it disappear into "just how this Sprint went."** Farah records the three days of now-obsolete work as real data — useful both for forecasting accuracy going forward and as evidence if this client's requirement instability needs to be raised as a pattern later.
7. **If this is the second or third time a specific source has blown up a Sprint Goal mid-flight, treat it as structural, not another one-off firefight.** Farah raises with Devon, and if needed with account management directly, whether earlier involvement of this client during refinement, or a standing risk buffer for their historically unstable requirements, would prevent the pattern instead of the team repeatedly absorbing it as pain.

## Likely Outcome

Devon doesn't cancel Sprint 22 — the adapted goal holds, the team ships the two completed items plus solid groundwork, and picks up the retry-logic work cleanly in Sprint 23 once the API documentation lands, costing roughly three developer-days net rather than the fuller re-planning overhead a formal cancellation would have added. The same-day transparency to stakeholders lands well — the client's account lead specifically appreciates hearing about the change immediately rather than discovering it at Review. The recurring-instability angle turns out to be real: this is the second time in four months this specific client has caused a goal-level disruption, which becomes the basis for Farah and Devon proposing a dedicated early-review step for this client's requirements in future refinement sessions.

## Certification Exam Angle

A typical framing: *"Mid-Sprint, a significant external change makes the Sprint Goal obsolete. Who decides whether to cancel the Sprint, and what should the Scrum Master's role be?"* Trap answers include the Scrum Master deciding whether to cancel the Sprint since they're responsible for the process (factually wrong and a frequently-tested fact — the Scrum Guide gives this authority to the Product Owner alone), the team pushing through and completing the originally planned items regardless because changing scope disrupts the Sprint (ignores that continuing toward a genuinely obsolete goal wastes effort rather than demonstrating discipline), or cancelling the Sprint any time significant scope changes are needed (over-applies cancellation to situations that only require in-Sprint adaptation, which is available far more often and far less disruptive than most people assume).

The correct-mindset answer knows precisely that only the Product Owner can cancel a Sprint, that cancellation is reserved for when the Sprint Goal is genuinely obsolete rather than merely in need of adjustment, and that the Scrum Master's role is to facilitate the Product Owner's decision-making and the team's re-planning either way — plus, at the meta level, to notice and address the pattern if this keeps recurring from the same root cause.
