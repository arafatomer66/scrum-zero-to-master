# A New Scrum Master's First 30 Days

*A developer has just been assigned to Scrum Master an existing team they've never worked closely with — what to observe before changing anything, who to build relationships with first, and what not to touch in week one.*

## The Situation

Zainab Hussain has spent four years as a backend engineer at Torrent Media, working on the recommendations pipeline. She studied for PSM I on her own and passed it eight months ago, and when the previous Scrum Master for Team Wavelength left the company, her manager asked her to take it on. Wavelength — seven people, building the personalization engine — isn't a team she's worked closely with before. She knows their reputation (competent, mostly self-sufficient) but not their history, their internal dynamics, or how their ceremonies actually function day to day.

Her manager's guidance on day one is thin: "Just keep the Sprints running, they're a pretty mature team." That's not nothing, but it's not much either. Zainab doesn't know if Wavelength's velocity of 45 reflects real capacity or padded estimates. She doesn't know whether their Daily Scrum is genuinely useful coordination or quiet theater. She doesn't know who on the team actually drives technical decisions versus who's simply present in the room. She has the title. She does not yet have any of the context, or the trust, that would let her use it well.

## Why This Happens

Being assigned to an existing team you didn't grow into is a common, structurally risky moment for a Scrum Master, and it carries specific traps most new Scrum Masters don't see coming:

- **Scrum Masters are frequently parachuted onto teams** through reorgs, a predecessor leaving, or a transfer, rather than growing into the role alongside a team they already know.
- **The temptation to prove value fast by visibly fixing something in week one nearly always backfires**, because an early fix usually addresses the new Scrum Master's own assumption, not the team's actual, lived problem.
- **Trust with a stranger takes time, and an early misstep costs disproportionately.** Calling out something the team considers normal, before understanding why they do it that way, can cost months of credibility.
- **"They're a mature team, just keep it running" is often incomplete guidance**, usually from a manager who isn't closely watching that team's retrospectives or reviews either — maturity in delivery doesn't guarantee maturity in every ceremony.
- **Having the title isn't the same as having earned standing to challenge the team.** New Scrum Masters often conflate the two, either overreaching on authority they haven't built yet or underreaching by assuming "mature" means "hands off entirely."

## The Scrum Master's Approach

**Week 1 — observe, don't touch.**

1. **Attend every ceremony as a quiet observer first.** Zainab sits in Daily Scrum and refinement without changing the format yet, even where she privately suspects the round-robin status updates look more like reporting than real coordination.
2. **Run a short 1:1 with every team member**, twenty to thirty minutes each, using the same three open questions across all seven: "What's working well right now?" "What's the most annoying friction in a normal week?" and "What do you wish the previous Scrum Master had done differently, or done at all?" She pattern-matches across answers rather than acting on any single opinion.
3. **Read the artifacts' real history, not just their current state.** She pulls the last six Sprints of velocity, checks how many Sprint Goals were actually achieved versus quietly abandoned, and reads a couple of archived retrospective boards to separate "mature and healthy" from "mature and coasting."
4. **Map the team's informal structure.** Who does the team actually defer to technically and socially — often not the person with the most senior title — matters enormously for who to bring along on any future change.

**Week 2 — build relationships deliberately.**

5. **Get real time with the Product Owner specifically.** Zainab asks how they see the team's health, what their own friction points are, and quietly assesses whether the Product Owner–Developer dynamic looks healthy or strained — usually the highest-leverage relationship for a Scrum Master to understand early.
6. **Find the team's informal technical leader and build a working alliance**, without it becoming visible favoritism — this is often the fastest route to an honest read on what's really happening, and a credible partner for piloting any future idea.
7. **Learn what's happening outside the Scrum ceremonies that still affects the team** — on-call rotations, cross-team dependencies, tooling pain — so she understands system-level constraints, not just what's visible inside the events themselves.

**Weeks 3–4 — form hypotheses, make one small move, resist the rest.**

8. **Synthesize the 1:1s and observation into two or three honest hypotheses** about where the real friction actually sits — for example, that Daily Scrum functions as status-to-Product-Owner rather than peer coordination, or that retro action items never get tracked past the meeting that creates them. She writes these down without acting on all of them yet.
9. **Pick exactly one, the smallest and least controversial, and propose it collaboratively rather than announce it.** "I noticed retro actions haven't had an owner tracked — want to try adding that for one Sprint and see if it helps?" is framed as a reversible experiment, the team's call to make.
10. **Explicitly resist overhauling anything else in month one**, even something that looks clearly broken, because a mature team's trust in a brand-new Scrum Master is fragile, and an early move that reads as "proving I'm needed" costs more credibility than most fixes are worth this early.
11. **Set a real thirty-day checkpoint with her own manager** to report what she's learned, not what she's changed, and to replace the vague "just keep it running" with something concrete both of them understand as success for month two.

## Likely Outcome

By day 30, Zainab has an evidence-based read on Wavelength: technically competent, but Daily Scrum has drifted into status theater aimed at the Product Owner — confirmed independently by five of seven 1:1s — and retro actions really do die on the whiteboard. She hasn't fixed either yet, deliberately. But she's earned enough trust through a quiet first month that when she proposes the retro-tracking experiment in week four, the team's informal technical lead, Kwame Asante, backs it openly in the room — support she almost certainly wouldn't have gotten proposing the same idea as a stranger in week one. The Daily Scrum drift waits another month, on purpose, until she has more standing.

## Certification Exam Angle

This scenario shows up in both PSM II and Scrum Alliance's A-CSM material, typically framed as: *"A Scrum Master is newly assigned to an established Scrum Team they have not worked with before. What should they do first?"* Trap answers include immediately auditing the team's ceremonies against strict Scrum Guide compliance and correcting any deviation found (ignores that a practice looking like a violation may be a deliberate, well-reasoned adaptation the new Scrum Master doesn't understand yet), waiting passively for the team to bring problems forward since they're "already mature" (underreaching — servant leadership isn't passive), or replicating what worked with a previous team to establish quick credibility (importing a solution before diagnosing this team's actual problem).

The correct-mindset answer prioritizes observation, relationship-building, and genuinely understanding a team's context and history before proposing any change — and even then, starting with the smallest reversible experiment rather than a redesign. This reflects both servant leadership and the [coaching stance](../04-scrum-master-role/coaching-vs-mentoring-vs-teaching.md) of drawing out a team's own capability rather than imposing an outside fix on day one.
