# Scaling From One Team to Five

*What started as a single seven-person Scrum Team has grown into five teams touching the same product, and integration conflicts are now a weekly occurrence.*

## The Situation

Eighteen months ago, Meridian Health's patient-portal product was one Scrum Team: seven people, one Product Backlog, one Sprint Review where the whole thing got demoed end to end. Growth has been fast — the product now has five Scrum Teams (Appointments, Messaging, Billing, Records, and a Platform team), roughly 35 engineers total, all still shipping against what is nominally "one product" but is now, in practice, five separately planned Sprint Backlogs pulling from five team-specific chunks of what used to be a single backlog.

In the last month alone: the Messaging team shipped a notification-preferences change that silently broke an assumption the Appointments team's reminder feature depended on, discovered only in production. Records and Billing both built overlapping logic for redacting a patient's data on account deletion, independently, because neither team's backlog referred to the other's work. There is no single Sprint Review anymore — each team demos separately, to different subsets of stakeholders, and nobody in the room has seen all five. The original Scrum Master, Aditi, who's stayed on as Appointments' Scrum Master, is fielding "why didn't we know about this" questions almost every week that have nothing to do with her own team's Sprint.

## Why This Happens

This is not a discipline failure inside any one team — every team listed above is, individually, running clean Scrum. The problem is that nobody has installed anything *above* the team level to coordinate the fact that five teams share one product, one codebase's worth of dependencies, and one customer-facing outcome:

- **Product Backlog ordering that made sense for one team doesn't automatically decompose cleanly across five.** When there was one backlog, one Product Owner's judgment call kept dependencies visible by default, because everything sat in the same list. Splitting the backlog across five teams without an explicit mechanism for surfacing cross-team dependencies means those dependencies now have to be *actively* found instead of being visible by default — and nobody assigned that job to anyone.
- **Five separate Sprint Reviews mean nobody holds the whole-product picture anymore.** A single integrated view of "what does the product do today, all together" quietly disappeared the moment Review split into five rooms, and the redaction-logic duplication is a direct, predictable consequence of that.
- **Growth from one team to five is a genuinely different problem than doing Scrum well within one team**, and organizations very often scale team count without scaling coordination structure — because adding a sixth Scrum Team feels like "more of the same thing that worked," when past a certain team count it isn't the same problem anymore, it's a scaling problem, which is a distinct discipline. See [why and when to scale](../05-scaling-frameworks/why-and-when-to-scale.md) for the specific signals that indicate a genuine scaling need versus a fixable single-team issue.
- **Nobody made the decision to scale — it happened by accretion**, one new team added at a time as headcount grew, each addition locally reasonable, with no single point where someone stopped and asked "does our cross-team coordination mechanism still work at this team count?"

## The Scrum Master's Approach

1. **Name the actual failure mode precisely, with the two recent incidents as evidence, before proposing any framework.** Aditi documents the Messaging/Appointments break and the Records/Billing duplication as concrete, dated examples of the same root cause — cross-team dependency and overlap invisible until it's already shipped — and brings this to the other four Scrum Masters and the Product Owners as a shared problem, not just her own team's complaint.
2. **Get the other Scrum Masters and Product Owners in the same room before reaching for a specific framework name.** Aditi's first move is a working session across all five teams: "We have five teams and effectively no mechanism for seeing dependencies or a single product view. What's actually true about our structure right now?" Naming the real coordination gaps first prevents adopting a framework's ceremonies as cargo-cult ritual without understanding what problem they solve.
3. **Evaluate whether a lightweight scaling framework fits, rather than assuming the biggest name is the right one.** Given that Meridian's five teams work on one product with a genuinely single Product Backlog conceptually (even if split for planning), Aditi researches [Nexus](../05-scaling-frameworks/nexus.md) — designed specifically for three-to-nine Scrum Teams working from one Product Backlog toward one Done Increment, with a Nexus Integration Team whose whole job is surfacing and resolving exactly the cross-team dependency and integration problems Meridian is hitting. She weighs it against heavier options and reports back why Nexus's scope (not SAFe's, not a from-scratch LeSS adoption) matches Meridian's actual size and structure.
4. **Propose a minimum viable coordination structure even before any framework is formally adopted**, because the two incidents are costing real money now. Aditi proposes a small cross-team integration group — one representative per team, meeting briefly a few times a week — whose explicit job is surfacing dependencies before they ship, essentially piloting the Nexus Integration Team concept without waiting for a full framework rollout to be approved.
5. **Reinstate a single, integrated view of the product**, even if the underlying planning stays team-by-team. Aditi pushes for one combined Sprint Review (or a structured refined process) where all five teams show a single integrated increment to a shared stakeholder audience, specifically so the whole-product picture Aditi lost is restored, and specifically so redaction-logic-style duplication becomes visible before it's built twice.
6. **Frame this to leadership as a structural investment, not a process complaint.** Aditi and the other Scrum Masters jointly present the cost of the status quo (a production incident, weeks of duplicated engineering effort) against the cost of piloting Nexus properly for one product cycle, making the trade-off a business decision leadership can actually weigh, rather than an internal team grievance that's easy to deprioritize.

## Likely Outcome

Leadership approves a Nexus pilot for one quarter rather than a full framework rollout across the whole engineering org, which is the right-sized bet — it tests the mechanism on the one product that actually needs it instead of imposing new ceremony on unrelated teams. The Nexus Integration Team, staffed initially by Aditi and one representative from each other team, catches two more potential cross-team conflicts in the first six weeks before they ship, which is enough evidence for the pilot to be extended. The combined Sprint Review takes real facilitation effort to keep from becoming five sequential fifteen-minute demos with no actual integration discussion — Aditi has to actively steer it toward "how does this fit together" rather than "here's what my team did," but by the third combined Review the habit starts to form on its own.

## Certification Exam Angle

A scaling-scenario question typically reads: *"A product has grown from one Scrum Team to five teams working on overlapping components, and integration conflicts are increasingly common. What should the Scrum Master do?"* The trap answer is often "each team should simply communicate more with each other informally" — this sounds reasonable but ignores that ad hoc communication is exactly what worked at one team and has already demonstrably failed at five; the exam is testing whether you recognize that team count crossing a threshold is a structural problem requiring a structural answer, not a discipline reminder.

A second trap answer: "immediately adopt SAFe to bring order across all the teams" — tempting because it's the most well-known scaling name, but it's usually the wrong-sized answer for a single product with five teams sharing one backlog; reaching for the heaviest framework without evaluating fit is its own anti-pattern. The correct-mindset answer recognizes the specific symptom (multiple teams, one product, one Product Backlog, integration and dependency problems) as the exact use case Nexus was designed for, and treats framework adoption as something to evaluate against the actual structural need — team count, backlog structure, dependency type — rather than a default reflex, consistent with [why and when to scale](../05-scaling-frameworks/why-and-when-to-scale.md) emphasizing that scaling frameworks solve a specific coordination problem and should be sized to it.
