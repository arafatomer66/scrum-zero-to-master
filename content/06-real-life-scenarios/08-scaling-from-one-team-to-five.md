# Scaling From One Team to Five

*What started as a single seven-person Scrum Team has grown into five teams touching the same product, and integration conflicts are now a weekly occurrence.*

## The Situation

Eighteen months ago, Meridian Health's patient-portal product was one Scrum Team: seven people, one Product Backlog, one Sprint Review where the whole thing got demoed end to end. Growth has been fast — the product now has five Scrum Teams (Appointments, Messaging, Billing, Records, and a Platform team), roughly 35 engineers total, all still shipping against what is nominally "one product" but is now, in practice, five separately planned Sprint Backlogs pulling from five team-specific chunks of what used to be a single backlog.

In the last month alone: the Messaging team shipped a notification-preferences change that silently broke an assumption the Appointments team's reminder feature depended on, discovered only in production. Records and Billing both built overlapping logic for redacting a patient's data on account deletion, independently, because neither team's backlog referred to the other's work. There is no single Sprint Review anymore — each team demos separately, to different subsets of stakeholders, and nobody in the room has seen all five. The original Scrum Master, Aditi, who's stayed on as Appointments' Scrum Master, is fielding "why didn't we know about this" questions almost every week that have nothing to do with her own team's Sprint.

## Why This Happens

This is not a discipline failure inside any one team — every team listed above is, individually, running clean Scrum. The problem is that nobody has installed anything *above* the team level to coordinate the fact that five teams share one product, one codebase's worth of dependencies, and one customer-facing outcome:

- **Product Backlog ordering that made sense for one team doesn't decompose cleanly across five.** With one backlog, one Product Owner's judgment kept dependencies visible by default. Split across five teams with no explicit mechanism for surfacing cross-team dependencies, those dependencies now have to be *actively* found — and nobody was assigned that job.
- **Five separate Sprint Reviews mean nobody holds the whole-product picture anymore.** A single integrated view of "what does the product do today, all together" quietly disappeared the moment Review split into five rooms, and the redaction-logic duplication is a direct, predictable consequence.
- **Growth from one team to five is a genuinely different problem than doing Scrum well within one team**, and organizations often scale team count without scaling coordination structure — adding a sixth team feels like "more of the same," when past a certain count it's a distinct discipline. See [why and when to scale](../05-scaling-frameworks/why-and-when-to-scale.md) for signals that indicate a genuine scaling need versus a fixable single-team issue.
- **Nobody made the decision to scale — it happened by accretion**, one team added at a time as headcount grew, each addition locally reasonable, with no single point where anyone asked "does our cross-team coordination mechanism still work at this count?"

## The Scrum Master's Approach

1. **Name the actual failure mode precisely, with the two recent incidents as evidence, before proposing any framework.** Aditi documents the Messaging/Appointments break and the Records/Billing duplication as concrete, dated examples of one root cause — cross-team dependency and overlap invisible until it's already shipped — and brings this to the other four Scrum Masters and Product Owners as a shared problem, not just her own team's complaint.
2. **Get the other Scrum Masters and Product Owners in the same room before reaching for a specific framework name.** Aditi's first move is a working session: "We have five teams and effectively no mechanism for seeing dependencies or a single product view. What's actually true about our structure right now?" Naming the real coordination gaps first prevents adopting a framework's ceremonies as cargo-cult ritual.
3. **Evaluate whether a lightweight scaling framework fits, rather than assuming the biggest name is right.** Given that Meridian's five teams work on one product with a conceptually single Product Backlog, Aditi researches [Nexus](../05-scaling-frameworks/nexus.md) — built for three-to-nine Scrum Teams working from one Product Backlog toward one Done Increment, with a Nexus Integration Team whose job is surfacing exactly the dependency and integration problems Meridian is hitting. She weighs it against heavier options and reports back why Nexus's scope, not SAFe's, matches Meridian's actual size.
4. **Propose a minimum viable coordination structure even before any framework is formally adopted**, because the incidents are costing real money now. Aditi proposes a small cross-team integration group — one representative per team, meeting briefly a few times a week — whose job is surfacing dependencies before they ship, essentially piloting the Nexus Integration Team concept early.
5. **Reinstate a single, integrated view of the product**, even if planning stays team-by-team. Aditi pushes for one combined Sprint Review where all five teams show a single integrated increment to a shared stakeholder audience, so the whole-product picture is restored and duplication becomes visible before it's built twice.
6. **Frame this to leadership as a structural investment, not a process complaint.** Aditi and the other Scrum Masters jointly present the cost of the status quo (a production incident, weeks of duplicated effort) against the cost of piloting Nexus for one product cycle, making it a business decision leadership can weigh.

## Likely Outcome

Leadership approves a Nexus pilot for one quarter rather than a full framework rollout across engineering, which is the right-sized bet — it tests the mechanism on the one product that needs it instead of imposing new ceremony on unrelated teams. The Nexus Integration Team, staffed by Aditi and one representative per team, catches two more potential cross-team conflicts in the first six weeks, enough evidence for the pilot to be extended. The combined Sprint Review takes real facilitation effort to avoid becoming five sequential demos with no actual integration discussion — Aditi steers it toward "how does this fit together," and by the third combined Review the habit starts to form on its own.

## Certification Exam Angle

A scaling-scenario question typically reads: *"A product has grown from one Scrum Team to five teams working on overlapping components, and integration conflicts are increasingly common. What should the Scrum Master do?"* The trap answer is often "each team should simply communicate more with each other informally" — this sounds reasonable but ignores that ad hoc communication is exactly what worked at one team and has already demonstrably failed at five; the exam is testing whether you recognize that team count crossing a threshold is a structural problem requiring a structural answer, not a discipline reminder.

A second trap answer: "immediately adopt SAFe to bring order across all the teams" — tempting because it's the most well-known scaling name, but it's usually the wrong-sized answer for a single product with five teams sharing one backlog; reaching for the heaviest framework without evaluating fit is its own anti-pattern. The correct-mindset answer recognizes the specific symptom (multiple teams, one product, one Product Backlog, integration and dependency problems) as the exact use case Nexus was designed for, and treats framework adoption as something to evaluate against the actual structural need — team count, backlog structure, dependency type — rather than a default reflex, consistent with [why and when to scale](../05-scaling-frameworks/why-and-when-to-scale.md) emphasizing that scaling frameworks solve a specific coordination problem and should be sized to it.
