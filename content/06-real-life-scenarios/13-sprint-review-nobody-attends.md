# When Stakeholders Stop Showing Up to Sprint Review

*The invite list hasn't changed, but attendance has quietly dropped to zero outsiders, and the team has started treating the ceremony as a demo to itself.*

## The Situation

Team Beacon builds the patient-scheduling module for Coral Health. Product Owner Wes Okafor, Scrum Master Ingrid Larsen, seven developers. A year ago, Sprint Review regularly drew six to eight stakeholders — a clinic operations manager, two nurses who used the scheduling tool daily, a billing lead. At Sprint 26's Review, the count is zero. Just the team, Wes, and Ingrid, going through the same thirty-minute format: developers click through completed tickets, Wes says "looks good, ship it," and nobody asks a question because nobody outside the team is in the room to ask one.

Checking the calendar afterward, Ingrid notices the invite still goes out every two weeks — it's just that three of the four regular stakeholders have started auto-declining or simply not responding. In the hallway a few days later, clinic operations manager Fatima Rahman tells Ingrid, without much drama: "Honestly, the demos never really showed us anything we could weigh in on. It always felt like it was already decided by the time we saw it."

## Why This Happens

A Sprint Review that stakeholders stop attending is almost never about stakeholders losing interest in the product — it's usually about the event having quietly stopped delivering what it promises:

- **The event became presentation theater instead of collaborative inspection.** If nothing a stakeholder says in the room ever visibly changes what happens next, attending starts to feel pointless, and people correctly stop investing their time in it.
- **What's demoed is too polished and too far downstream to invite real feedback.** By the time work is "done" enough to show, direction has often already been locked in — which is exactly what Fatima meant by "already decided."
- **The feedback loop is invisible even when it does close.** If stakeholders never see their previous input reflected back — "you said X last time, here's what changed because of it" — they have no evidence their attendance mattered, even in cases where it genuinely did.
- **Fixed scheduling stops accommodating the people it's meant to serve.** A recurring slot that was convenient a year ago may no longer fit a clinic operations manager's shifting schedule, and nobody revisited it.
- **The team adapts to the emptiness rather than fighting it**, which is a rational but self-reinforcing response — an internal-only demo is easier to prepare for than one with real stakeholder scrutiny, so the format drifts further from genuine inspection each cycle.

## The Scrum Master's Approach

1. **Ask the missing stakeholders directly, rather than guess.** Ingrid runs short fifteen-minute conversations with three lapsed regulars, including Fatima, asking plainly what would make Sprint Review worth their time again.
2. **Reframe the event's purpose internally before changing anything external.** Ingrid coaches Wes that [Sprint Review](../02-scrum-framework/sprint-review.md) exists for collaborative inspection and adaptation of the product, not a status presentation — and helps him shift from narrating a slide-style walkthrough to opening with "here's what we learned, here's what we're deciding next," genuinely inviting reaction.
3. **Close the feedback loop visibly, every single time.** The new first agenda item, every Review, is "last time Fatima raised X — here's what we did about it." Stakeholders need to see their input land somewhere concrete before they'll trust that showing up again is worth it.
4. **Bring work earlier and rougher, not just more polished.** Ingrid coaches the team to demo real work-in-progress when a genuine direction decision is still open, rather than only shippable, decided-already increments — inviting input while it can still change something.
5. **Fix the logistics that are actually in the way.** The Review slot moves to better match the clinic team's shift schedule, and the agenda tightens so attending costs stakeholders less time for the value they get back.
6. **Send targeted, specific invitations instead of a recurring generic one.** Instead of a standing calendar hold nobody reads closely, Wes sends Fatima a direct note: "We're deciding between two approaches to the intake flow next Sprint — would love your read on this one specifically."
7. **Track attendance and the quality of the dialogue over several Sprints, and escalate if it's structural.** If engagement still doesn't recover after real changes, Ingrid treats that as a signal worth raising as an organizational question — for instance, if stakeholders have been reallocated away from this product entirely — rather than something to keep chasing indefinitely at the team level.

## Likely Outcome

Fatima returns for Sprint 28's Review after being asked a specific, pointed question about the intake flow, and her feedback changes the ordering of two backlog items live in the meeting — the first time in months the Review has produced a visible decision. Attendance climbs back to two or three regulars rather than the original six to eight; some stakeholders really have moved on to other projects and Ingrid stops chasing them once that's confirmed rather than treating headcount as the goal. The real measure of success isn't the number of people in the room — it's that the Review starts producing genuine backlog changes again, which it hadn't in months.

## Certification Exam Angle

A typical framing: *"Stakeholders have stopped attending Sprint Review, and the Development Team now demos completed work only to the Product Owner. What should the Scrum Master do?"* Trap answers include canceling the Sprint Review since stakeholders aren't interested (this destroys the actual empirical checkpoint the team most needs, rather than diagnosing why it stopped working), making attendance mandatory through a top-down policy (compliance without genuine value doesn't restore real feedback, and the Scrum Master has no authority over stakeholders' calendars regardless), or having the Scrum Master personally present the work to stakeholders on the team's behalf (removes the team from the direct feedback loop entirely, defeating the point of collaborative inspection).

The correct-mindset answer is to understand that Sprint Review's purpose is genuine inspection and adaptation of the product increment together with stakeholders, diagnose why that feedback loop broke, and rebuild the event so it earns attendance back by delivering real value — rather than legislating attendance or abandoning the event.
