# The Team Wants to Drop Scrum Entirely

*After a brutal quarter, it isn't leadership pushing back on the framework — it's the team itself, telling the Scrum Master the ceremonies feel like pure overhead with nothing to show for them.*

## The Situation

Team Ferrous builds ERP integrations at Kestrel Manufacturing Software — six developers, Scrum Master Owen Baptiste, Product Owner Lin Cho. The last quarter has been brutal: a major client escalation consumed three Sprints of firefighting, and the team has missed its Sprint Goal twice in the process. Morale is low and everyone knows it.

In the Sprint 33 retrospective, senior developer Halima Aziz says out loud what several others have clearly been thinking: "Can we just... stop doing this? Standups feel like a status meeting nobody needs. Retro hasn't produced anything real in two months. Planning takes ninety minutes to plan work that changes by Wednesday anyway. Let's just run a Kanban board and Slack and get back to actually shipping." A few heads nod around the table. This isn't leadership mandating a change from above — it's the team Owen is supposed to be serving, exhausted, asking to abandon the framework he's there to champion. He feels the reflex to defend Scrum rise immediately, and recognizes that reflex, right now, would ring completely hollow.

## Why This Happens

- **During sustained crisis, any ceremony whose value hasn't been recently, concretely felt is the first reasonable thing to want to cut.** If the team never connected retro or planning to a specific win, the cost of continuing them starts to feel unjustifiable the moment things get hard.
- **"Ceremony fatigue" is often a symptom of ceremonies that degraded into theater**, not evidence that empiricism itself doesn't work. The team is reacting to bad execution — a status-report standup, a retro with no real action-tracking — but doesn't always distinguish that from the framework's actual value proposition.
- **A genuinely rough quarter erodes trust that the process is helping**, especially when nobody has recently reconnected the ceremonies to a concrete outcome the team can point to and say "that mattered."
- **"Just use Kanban and Slack" is sometimes a legitimate signal**, not only a tired complaint — a team whose actual work pattern has become interrupt-driven, client-facing firefighting may genuinely be better served by flow-based practices blended in, not by dismissing the request outright.

## The Scrum Master's Approach

1. **Don't defend the framework reflexively — listen and validate what's true first.** Owen's first move is acknowledging the real parts of the complaint before responding to any of it: "You're right that retro hasn't produced a real change in two months. That's a genuine problem, whether or not it's a Scrum problem specifically — let's not skip past it."
2. **Separate the complaint into its actual components instead of treating "abandon Scrum" as one atomic ask.** Owen distinguishes ceremonies that have genuinely degraded into theater (retro, possibly planning) from ceremonies whose value was simply never made visible (standup) from a real structural mismatch (interrupt-driven client work against fixed-length Sprints).
3. **Bring data, not defensiveness, about what actually happened this quarter.** Owen shows the team, plainly, that most of the quarter's pain traces to an unplanned client escalation eating capacity — not to the framework itself — and gently names that conflating "we had a brutal quarter" with "Scrum failed us" deserves a second look.
4. **Take the "just use Kanban" idea seriously instead of dismissing it.** Owen genuinely explores with the team whether their current work pattern might benefit from flow-based elements blended into how they work — a legitimate empirical adaptation, not a defeat, and not something to shut down to protect the framework's reputation.
5. **Propose a real, time-boxed experiment instead of an all-or-nothing decision.** "Let's run one Sprint with a stripped-down format — a ten-minute standup focused strictly on blockers, and a retro with one rule: every action item gets an owner and gets checked at the start of the next retro, no exceptions. If it still feels like theater after that, we talk seriously about bigger changes."
6. **Fix the concrete, legitimate complaints regardless of how the bigger debate resolves.** "Retro produces nothing" is fixable independent of the framework question — tighter facilitation and tracked actions change the conversation's premise on their own.
7. **Revisit the decision explicitly after the experiment, as the team's decision, not Owen's.** If, after a genuinely well-run Sprint, the team still wants to drop some or all of the ceremonies, Owen treats that as legitimate inspect-and-adapt at the team level and helps them land on what actually serves them — rather than treating "staying in Scrum" as the only acceptable outcome of his own coaching.

## Likely Outcome

The stripped-down Sprint 34 experiment lands better than expected. The ten-minute, blockers-only standup gets genuine relief from three developers who'd been dreading the old format, and the tracked-action retro produces two real fixes that visibly stick through Sprint 35. Halima, the loudest voice for abandoning Scrum two Sprints earlier, admits at the Sprint 35 retro: "Okay, that actually felt useful. I take it back." The team doesn't abandon Scrum, but it does permanently keep the tighter formats rather than reverting to the bloated versions — the framework surviving specifically because Owen let it be genuinely questioned instead of defended on reflex. Not every version of this resolves this cleanly: sometimes the honest answer really is that a support-heavy team is better served by a Kanban-flavored approach, and a good Scrum Master lets that be true too.

## Certification Exam Angle

This scenario is favored in Scrum Alliance's A-CSM material and PSM II, both of which weight judgment over recall: *"After a difficult quarter, the Scrum Team asks the Scrum Master to stop holding Sprint Retrospectives and Daily Scrums, saying they add no value. What should the Scrum Master do?"* Trap answers include refusing outright and insisting the events continue exactly as prescribed because the Scrum Guide requires them (rigid and dogmatic — it ignores that the complaint may legitimately be about execution quality rather than the event's actual purpose, and this reflex is heavily penalized on real PSM II questions), immediately agreeing to drop the events since the team is self-managing and gets to decide (abdicates the Scrum Master's own accountability to uphold Scrum's value — self-management doesn't mean staying silent while a team discards something that could still serve it if fixed), or quietly continuing the events under different names so the team doesn't notice (dishonest, undermines transparency, and doesn't address the real complaint at all).

The correct-mindset answer takes the complaint seriously as real signal, distinguishes poor execution from the event's actual purpose, and uses genuine inspect-and-adapt — including real, time-boxed experiments — to either fix or legitimately evolve the team's practice, treating the team's voice as data rather than a threat to be managed.
