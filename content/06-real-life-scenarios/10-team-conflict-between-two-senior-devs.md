# Team Conflict Between Two Senior Developers

*Two senior engineers have been at odds for months — technically and personally — and it's started costing the team real delivery time.*

## The Situation

Team Osprey, building the pricing engine for a travel-booking platform, has two senior developers, Ravindra and Callie, who have been on the team since its founding three years ago. They disagree, fundamentally, about how the service layer should be structured — Ravindra favors a strict layered architecture, Callie favors a more pragmatic, feature-driven approach that ships faster but accumulates more inconsistency. This used to be a healthy, occasionally sharp, technical debate. Over the last two months it's become personal.

In a recent code review, Callie left a comment on Ravindra's pull request: "This is the same over-engineered pattern that took us three extra days last Sprint. Can we just ship things for once?" Ravindra didn't reply in the thread — he brought it to the Scrum Master, Nkechi, privately, visibly upset, and said he's started deliberately avoiding pairing with Callie. Two junior developers have started quietly asking Nkechi which of the two they should align with on an upcoming design decision, which tells Nkechi the conflict has started splitting the team into camps rather than staying contained between the two of them. Sprint 22's velocity dropped to 24 points against a rolling average of 34, and both Ravindra and Callie separately cite "waiting on the other" as a factor.

## Why This Happens

Conflict between two senior, high-context people is common precisely because seniority means strong, well-earned opinions, and long tenure means the disagreement has had years to compound past its original technical content:

- **A genuine technical disagreement, left unresolved long enough, stops being about the technical question at all.** The layered-architecture-versus-pragmatic-shipping debate is a real, legitimate trade-off with no single correct answer — but three years of it never being explicitly decided means every new instance reopens the same wound instead of building on a prior resolution.
- **Public friction (a sharp code review comment) is often the visible tip of private frustration that's been building for a long time.** Callie's comment reads as sudden to an outside observer, but it's rarely actually sudden — it's usually the first time frustration leaked into a channel other people could see.
- **Unresolved senior conflict doesn't stay contained — it recruits.** Junior developers, uncertain how to navigate two respected seniors disagreeing, start reading social cues about who to align with, which fragments the team's ability to make design decisions as a group and quietly erodes psychological safety for anyone who isn't sure which "side" is safer to be on.
- **Velocity drops are a lagging, not leading, indicator.** By the time the sprint numbers show it, the actual damage — avoided pairing, withheld feedback, a team quietly splitting into camps — has usually been accumulating for weeks already.

## The Scrum Master's Approach

1. **Talk to each of them separately first, and listen for both the technical and the personal thread.** Nkechi meets with Ravindra, then Callie, individually, asking open questions rather than presenting a conclusion: "What's actually going on for you in this?" Ravindra reveals he feels his input gets dismissed as "over-engineering" without real engagement with the reasoning; Callie reveals she feels constant pressure from stakeholders to ship faster and experiences Ravindra's approach as not accounting for that pressure at all. Both concerns are legitimate — this isn't a case of one person being simply right.
2. **Do not try to solve the technical disagreement for them.** Nkechi is explicit with herself that deciding "layered architecture wins" or "pragmatic wins" is not her call to make and wouldn't fix the actual problem even if she had the standing to make it — the real issue is that the two of them have no functioning process for resolving a disagreement like this, so it just accumulates instead of ever closing.
3. **Bring them together for a structured conversation, not an ambush.** Nkechi sets up a private session with both of them, framed clearly in advance: "This isn't about deciding who's right on architecture — it's about how the two of you disagree productively, because right now it's affecting the whole team." She uses a structured approach — each person states the other's position back in their own words before responding — specifically to slow down the reflexive defensiveness that's built up. This is core [conflict resolution](../04-scrum-master-role/conflict-resolution.md) facilitation: creating a container safe enough for both people to be heard before either tries to win.
4. **Get them to a concrete, team-visible resolution mechanism for the underlying technical question**, not just an apology. Nkechi proposes the team adopt a lightweight architecture decision record process — significant structural disagreements get written up with trade-offs and decided as a team, with the decision documented, so the same fight doesn't have to be refought from scratch every time it resurfaces. This gives Ravindra and Callie's legitimate disagreement an actual outlet that doesn't route through interpersonal friction.
5. **Address the team-splitting directly, separately from the two-person conflict.** Nkechi talks to the junior developers who've been asking which side to align with and tells them plainly that they don't need to pick one — and she raises, gently, in a team setting, that design disagreements are healthy and the team's job is to work through them together, not to form camps around individuals.
6. **Watch delivery data as a signal, and follow up explicitly rather than assuming one conversation fixed it.** Nkechi checks in with both Ravindra and Callie individually a week later, and watches whether Sprint 23's velocity and pairing patterns actually shift, rather than declaring the issue closed after the one facilitated session.

## Likely Outcome

The facilitated conversation is tense for the first fifteen minutes but genuinely lands — both admit, once they've each had to restate the other's position, that they'd stopped actually listening months ago. The architecture-decision-record process gets adopted team-wide and turns out to be useful beyond this one conflict, giving the team a real mechanism for exactly the kind of disagreement that used to fester. Ravindra and Callie don't become close, and don't need to — but code review tone visibly improves within two Sprints, and Sprint 23's velocity recovers to 31 points. One junior developer tells Nkechi privately that the team "feels normal again," which is the clearest signal the camp-forming has stopped.

## Certification Exam Angle

A common situational question: *"Two senior Developers have an ongoing conflict that is affecting team dynamics and delivery. What is the Scrum Master's best course of action?"* The trap answer is often "escalate to their manager to resolve the personnel issue" — tempting because it feels appropriately serious, but it bypasses the Scrum Master's actual facilitative role and treats a team dynamics problem as a disciplinary one before any attempt at facilitation has even been tried, which is premature and can damage trust further.

A second trap answer: "the Scrum Master should decide the technical disagreement to remove the source of conflict" — this misunderstands the root cause; the technical question is a legitimate trade-off, not the actual thing that needs fixing, and a Scrum Master ruling on architecture oversteps a role that has no authority over technical decisions in the first place. The correct-mindset answer has the Scrum Master facilitate the conflict directly — using structured techniques to ensure both parties are heard, helping the team build a durable mechanism for resolving the underlying disagreement, and addressing the wider team impact (the camp-forming) separately from the two-person relationship — because conflict resolution and protecting team dynamics are core Scrum Master accountabilities, to be handled through facilitation and coaching, not delegated upward at the first sign of difficulty.
