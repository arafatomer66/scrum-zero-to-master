# Sprint Planning

*The event that kicks off the Sprint by answering three questions in order — why, what, and how — timeboxed to eight hours for a one-month Sprint.*

Sprint Planning initiates the [Sprint](sprint.md) by laying out the work to be performed for the Sprint ahead. The resulting plan is created by the collaborative work of the entire Scrum Team — this is not the Product Owner handing Developers a list, and it's not Developers picking tickets in isolation. All three accountabilities described in [The Scrum Team](scrum-team.md) are actively involved.

The Product Owner comes in prepared to discuss the most important Product Backlog items and how they map to the Product Goal — that preparation is what makes the whole event work; a Product Owner who shows up without a clear view of what matters most turns Sprint Planning into a slow negotiation instead of a fast one. The Scrum Team may invite other people to attend and provide advice, but the plan itself belongs to the Scrum Team.

## Timebox

Eight hours for a one-month Sprint. Shorter Sprints get a proportionally shorter event — a two-week Sprint typically runs a Sprint Planning of around four hours, though the Guide doesn't hand you an exact formula beyond "usually shorter for shorter Sprints." A well-run Planning for an experienced team routinely finishes well under the timebox; the ceiling exists to bound the worst case, not to be a target.

## Topic one: why is this Sprint valuable?

The Product Owner proposes how the product could increase its value and utility in the current Sprint. The whole Scrum Team then collaborates to define a Sprint Goal — a single objective that communicates why this Sprint matters, both to the team and to stakeholders who won't be in the room day to day. The Sprint Goal must be finalized before Sprint Planning ends; it's not something to leave fuzzy and hope clarifies itself later.

A good Sprint Goal reads as a sentence about outcome, not a list of tickets — "Let existing customers export their data as CSV" rather than "Do tickets 412, 415, and 420." The list of tickets is Topic Two's job. Topic One's job is making sure everyone in the room, and everyone reading the [Sprint Backlog](sprint-backlog.md) afterward, understands what the Sprint is *for*.

## Topic two: what can be Done this Sprint?

Through discussion with the Product Owner, the Developers select items from the [Product Backlog](product-backlog.md) to include in the Sprint. How many items get selected is entirely up to the Developers — no one else, not the Product Owner and not a manager, gets to force additional scope into the Sprint once the Developers have made their forecast. Items may get refined further during this conversation, which is normal and often increases everyone's confidence in the plan.

Forecasting how much fits is genuinely hard, and the Guide is honest about that. The more Developers know about their own past performance, their upcoming capacity, and their [Definition of Done](definition-of-done.md), the more confidence they can put behind the forecast — this is why teams with a stable, well-understood DoD tend to plan more accurately than teams still negotiating what "done" means sprint to sprint.

## Topic three: how will the chosen work get done?

For each selected Product Backlog item, the Developers plan the work necessary to create an Increment that meets the Definition of Done. This is often done by breaking items down into smaller pieces of a day or less of work. How this decomposition happens is entirely at the Developers' discretion — nobody outside the Developers dictates the technical approach or the task breakdown. By the end of Sprint Planning, the Developers should be able to explain to the Product Owner and Scrum Master how they intend to work together to accomplish the Sprint Goal, even if every individual task isn't planned out yet.

## The output

The Sprint Goal, the set of Product Backlog items selected, and the plan for delivering them together become the [Sprint Backlog](sprint-backlog.md). It's worth being precise about that: Sprint Backlog isn't a separate artifact someone writes up after the meeting — it *is* the output of these three topics, owned from that point forward entirely by the Developers.

## Who attends and how

The full Scrum Team — Product Owner, Scrum Master, and Developers — attends, with the Product Owner primarily driving Topics One and Two and the Developers driving Topic Three. The Scrum Master's role is to make sure the event happens, stays within its timebox, and stays productive — coaching the team on keeping Planning efficient rather than running it as a status meeting. Stakeholders or subject-matter experts can be invited for advice on specific items, but they don't have a vote in what gets selected or how it gets planned; that would violate the Developers' and Product Owner's accountabilities described in [The Scrum Team](scrum-team.md). A ready-to-use agenda for structuring this event is in [Sprint Planning Agenda](../08-templates-and-toolkit/sprint-planning-agenda.md).

## The common dysfunction

The most frequent failure mode is skipping or rushing Topic One and treating Sprint Planning as pure capacity allocation — just picking tickets off the top of the backlog until some velocity number is hit. It looks efficient, but it produces a Sprint Backlog with no unifying goal behind it. The damage shows up downstream, not in the meeting itself: the [Daily Scrum](daily-scrum.md) has nothing coherent to inspect progress against beyond "are the tickets moving," and the [Sprint Review](sprint-review.md) has no story to tell stakeholders beyond a list of completed tickets. A Sprint Goal isn't a formality tacked onto capacity planning — it's the thing that makes every later event in the Sprint mean something.
