# Sprint Retrospective

*The last event of the Sprint — inspecting how the Sprint went for people, relationships, process, and tools, and leaving with at least one real change for the next Sprint.*

The Sprint Retrospective's purpose is to plan ways to increase quality and effectiveness. The Scrum Team inspects how the last Sprint went with regard to individuals, interactions, processes, tools, and its [Definition of Done](definition-of-done.md) — and it's one of the only points in the framework where the Definition of Done itself is explicitly on the table for revision, if the team decides increasing it will improve quality going forward.

## What's inspected

The team discusses what went well during the Sprint, what problems it ran into, and how those problems were solved — or weren't. From that discussion, the Scrum Team identifies the most helpful changes to improve its own effectiveness, and those improvements can be addressed as soon as they're identified rather than waiting for a formal kickoff; some may get added directly to the next [Sprint Backlog](sprint-backlog.md) as part of the plan.

Note the scope: "individuals, interactions, processes, tools" is deliberately broader than "what tickets didn't get done." A Retrospective that only ever talks about tickets is missing most of what it's for — a recurring interpersonal friction, a tool that's slowing everyone down, or a process step nobody actually believes in are all fair, central territory for this event.

## Timebox and placement

Three hours for a one-month Sprint, shorter for shorter Sprints. It's the last event of the Sprint — it happens after the [Sprint Review](sprint-review.md) and formally concludes the Sprint before the next one begins immediately after.

## Why it's the most commonly skipped or rushed event

Of all five Scrum events, the Retrospective is the one teams cut first under pressure, and it's worth being honest about why, because the reasons are structural, not just laziness.

It produces no external deliverable — no stakeholder is in the room, nothing demoable comes out of it, so skipping it has no visible consequence to anyone outside the team. It lands at the exact moment the team is most eager to already be working on the next Sprint, so it competes directly against the felt urgency of a backlog that never stops refilling. And its payoff is deferred and hard to attribute — a good Retrospective this Sprint often pays off two or three Sprints later as a problem that quietly stopped recurring, which is much less visible than the cost of the meeting itself was in the moment. Under delivery pressure, "we already know how we're doing, let's just get back to work" feels like a reasonable trade. It's exactly backwards.

The cost of skipping it is specific: the [Daily Scrum](daily-scrum.md) can surface that a problem exists, but it has no timebox and no mandate to actually solve it — that's not what it's for. The Retrospective is the only event in the framework actually built with the time and structure to root-cause something recurring. Skip it, or consistently rush it to twenty distracted minutes, and a team accumulates the same friction Sprint after Sprint with literally no mechanism left to change it. That's not a minor inefficiency — it's [Adaptation](scrum-theory-pillars.md) quietly failing on a recurring basis, which defeats empiricism at its root. Inspection (noticing the problem exists) can happen informally anywhere; a real Retrospective is what turns noticing into a plan.

## What makes it actually work

A Retrospective's value depends almost entirely on whether people are being honest in the room, which means it depends directly on [Openness and Courage](scrum-values.md) — a team that's polite but not honest will produce a Retrospective full of safe, comfortable observations and nothing that actually changes. The Scrum Master's job here isn't to run the meeting from the front every time; it's to keep it psychologically safe enough that the true, awkward thing gets said, and then to make sure at least one concrete change actually survives past the meeting into the next Sprint. Techniques for facilitating that honestly — without it turning into either a complaint session or a blame session — are covered in [Facilitation Techniques](../04-scrum-master-role/facilitation-techniques.md).

Running the same format every single Sprint is one of the quieter ways a Retrospective goes stale — people start pattern-matching the questions before they're even asked, and the honest answers get shorter each time. A rotating set of formats, matched to what the team actually needs to talk about that Sprint, is covered in [Retrospective Formats](../08-templates-and-toolkit/retrospective-formats.md) — it's worth treating format choice as a real facilitation decision, not a default to leave on autopilot.

## The connection to Definition of Done

Because the Retrospective is explicitly where the team can decide to raise its [Definition of Done](definition-of-done.md), it's the natural place a team's quality bar actually evolves — a production incident traced to a missing check becomes, at the next Retrospective, a new line item added to DoD going forward. That evolution doesn't happen automatically; it happens because someone in the room used the Retrospective for what it's for, instead of treating it as a fifteen-minute formality wedged in before everyone logs off.
