# The Sprint Backlog

*The Developers' own real-time plan for the Sprint — Sprint Goal, the items pulled to achieve it, and the actionable plan for delivering them — owned entirely by Developers.*

The Sprint Backlog is composed of three things, produced together during [Sprint Planning](sprint-planning.md): the Sprint Goal (why this Sprint matters), the set of [Product Backlog](product-backlog.md) items selected for the Sprint (what will be worked on), and an actionable plan for delivering the Increment (how the Developers intend to do it). All three together are the Sprint Backlog — it isn't a separate document someone writes up afterward, it's the direct output of the three Sprint Planning topics.

## Who owns it

It belongs solely to the Developers. This is one of the sharpest ownership lines in the whole framework: the Sprint Backlog is a highly visible, real-time picture of the work the Developers plan to accomplish during the Sprint, and only the Developers can change it during the Sprint. Neither the Product Owner nor the Scrum Master edits it, reassigns items within it, or adds work to it unilaterally — if new work needs to enter the Sprint, that happens through the Developers deciding to take it on, informed by a conversation with the Product Owner, not by someone editing the board over their heads.

## Why it's emergent during the Sprint

As Developers work through the plan, they learn more about exactly what's needed to reach the Sprint Goal — that's expected, not a sign planning failed. As new work turns out to be necessary, Developers add it to the Sprint Backlog. As work gets done, the estimated remaining work is updated to reflect reality. And if part of the original plan turns out to be unnecessary to the Sprint Goal, Developers can remove it. This is what "actionable plan" means in practice: not a static list frozen at the start of the Sprint, but a living plan that stays honest about where things actually stand, updated by the people doing the work.

That's also why it needs to carry enough detail to support the [Daily Scrum](daily-scrum.md) — the whole point of that daily inspection is looking at real, current state, and the Sprint Backlog is what makes that possible. A Sprint Backlog that only gets updated once a week can't support a meaningful Daily Scrum; there's nothing accurate to look at.

## How it differs from the Product Backlog

It's worth being precise about the boundary, because the two get conflated constantly in casual conversation. The [Product Backlog](product-backlog.md) is the Product Owner's ordered list of everything that might ever be built for the product — it's long-lived, spans the whole life of the product, and is never finished. The Sprint Backlog is the Developers' committed, in-flight plan for *this Sprint only* — a subset of items pulled from the Product Backlog, plus the Sprint Goal that gives them a shared purpose, plus the delivery plan the Developers built for them. A Product Backlog item doesn't become part of the Sprint Backlog just by existing near the top of the list; it becomes part of it the moment Sprint Planning selects it and the Developers plan how they'll deliver it.

## The Sprint Goal's role inside it

The Sprint Goal isn't just the first line of the Sprint Backlog — it's the thing the rest of the plan is in service of, and it's the reference point Developers use when [renegotiating scope mid-Sprint](sprint.md). If a selected item turns out to not actually be necessary to achieve the Sprint Goal, Developers can drop or swap it in conversation with the Product Owner without that being a violation of anything — what can't move is the Sprint Goal itself. A Sprint Backlog that loses its Sprint Goal, or was built without a real one in the first place, degrades into a plain task list with nothing tying the items together, which is exactly the [Sprint Planning dysfunction](sprint-planning.md) worth watching for.

## What it looks like in practice

The Guide doesn't prescribe a tool or a visual format — a Kanban board, a Jira sprint board, a physical wall of index cards, and a shared spreadsheet have all been used to represent a Sprint Backlog. What's prescribed is the property it has to have: highly visible, real-time, and actionable, so that anyone looking at it — the team itself first, but also the Product Owner and any interested stakeholder — gets an honest, current picture of the Sprint without needing someone to translate it for them. That property is exactly what [Transparency](scrum-theory-pillars.md) requires of it.
