# Daily Scrum

*A 15-minute, Developer-led event to inspect progress toward the Sprint Goal and adapt the next day's plan — not a status report to the Scrum Master or a manager.*

The Daily Scrum's purpose is narrow and specific: inspect progress toward the Sprint Goal and adapt the [Sprint Backlog](sprint-backlog.md) as necessary, adjusting the upcoming work. It happens every working day of the Sprint, ideally at the same time and place to reduce complexity, and it's timeboxed to 15 minutes no matter how long the Sprint itself runs.

## Who it belongs to

The Daily Scrum is held by the Developers. If the Product Owner or Scrum Master are actively doing hands-on work on items in the Sprint Backlog, they participate as Developers for that purpose — but the event exists for the Developers to inspect their own progress, not for anyone to report to.

The 2020 Guide deliberately doesn't prescribe a format. Earlier versions of Scrum guidance were built around a fixed structure of three questions — what did you do yesterday, what will you do today, what's blocking you — asked of each person in turn. That format is now considered legacy; the current Guide gives the Developers latitude to run the event however works, as long as it stays focused on progress toward the Sprint Goal and produces an actionable plan for the next day. Walking the [Sprint Backlog](sprint-backlog.md) board item by item, discussing what's most at risk of not getting done, or focusing purely on what's blocking the Sprint Goal are all legitimate structures — the format is a means, the inspection is the end. The historical context for why the three-questions format faded out belongs in [the history of Scrum](../01-agile-foundations/history-of-scrum.md); a Scrum Master who still runs the old round-robin format by default is running a deprecated pattern, not a required one.

The Daily Scrum isn't the only point in the day Developers are allowed to adjust their plan — many teams meet again in smaller groups for more detailed technical discussion. The Daily Scrum is the guaranteed, whole-team checkpoint; it isn't meant to be the only conversation that happens.

## The Scrum Master's actual role in it

The Scrum Master ensures the Developers have the meeting and coaches them to keep it useful and within its timebox — that's the extent of the accountability. The Scrum Master does not run the Daily Scrum, does not call on people one at a time, and does not collect status. If a team is used to a Scrum Master facilitating every word of it, the healthiest move is often for the Scrum Master to deliberately step back and let the Developers own the room, coaching from the sideline rather than the center. This connects directly to [Servant Leadership](../04-scrum-master-role/servant-leadership.md) — service here looks like making yourself unnecessary to the meeting, not indispensable to it.

## The dysfunction: status report to a manager

The single most common way a Daily Scrum breaks is drifting into a status report — to the Scrum Master, or worse, to a manager or Product Owner sitting in on it as an evaluator. The symptoms are recognizable once you know to look for them: Developers address their updates to one person in the room instead of to each other, the "conversation" is really a sequence of individual monologues, nothing about the day's plan actually changes as a result of the meeting, and people quietly avoid mentioning real blockers because admitting a blocker feels like admitting failure in front of whoever's watching.

This violates the event's whole purpose, not just its tone. The point of the Daily Scrum isn't to move information upward — it's for the team to look at its own board, honestly, and decide what to do differently today because of what it sees. A status-report Daily Scrum still checks the calendar box, still runs for roughly 15 minutes, and still produces zero adaptation, because nobody in the room is actually using it to change the plan — they're using it to survive being watched. That's [Inspection without Adaptation](scrum-theory-pillars.md), which the Guide considers essentially pointless.

This exact failure pattern — and how a Scrum Master actually pulls a team out of it once it's set in — is worked through end to end in [Daily Scrum Becomes a Status Report to a Manager](../06-real-life-scenarios/03-daily-scrum-becomes-status-report-to-manager.md). The short version: the fix is rarely a stern reminder about "who this meeting is for." It's usually structural — physically or logically removing the manager from being the meeting's audience, changing who speaks first, or changing the format entirely so status-reporting isn't even the easy default. Specific techniques for that live in [Facilitation Techniques](../04-scrum-master-role/facilitation-techniques.md).

## What it's actually for, restated

Done well, the Daily Scrum improves communication inside the team, surfaces impediments while they're still small, promotes fast decisions about the day's work, and reduces the need for other status meetings entirely — because the information those meetings would have existed to collect is already flowing, daily, inside the team itself. That last point is worth stating plainly to skeptical stakeholders: a Daily Scrum that's actually working *replaces* status meetings elsewhere in the organization. One that's degraded into a status meeting itself has become exactly the overhead it was supposed to eliminate.
