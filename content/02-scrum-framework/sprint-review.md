# Sprint Review

*The event where the Scrum Team and stakeholders inspect the Increment together and adapt the Product Backlog — working software, not a slide deck, and never just a demo.*

Sprint Review's purpose is to inspect the outcome of the Sprint and determine what should change as a result. The Scrum Team presents the results of its work to key stakeholders, and progress toward the Product Goal is discussed openly. It's the second-to-last event of the Sprint, held before the [Sprint Retrospective](sprint-retrospective.md), and timeboxed to four hours for a one-month Sprint, shorter for shorter Sprints.

## What actually happens in the room

The Scrum Team and stakeholders review what was accomplished during the Sprint and what has changed in their environment since the last one — markets shift, competitors ship things, priorities move, and Sprint Review is where that outside context gets reconciled against what the team just built. The Scrum Team discusses what went well, what problems came up, and how those problems were handled. The [Increment](increment.md) itself gets demonstrated, and the discussion covers progress toward the Product Goal specifically, not just "here's what we finished." The group looks at timeline, budget, and the potential capabilities and marketplace for likely future releases. Based on all of that, the [Product Backlog](product-backlog.md) may be adjusted to reflect new opportunities — the result of a good Sprint Review is a revised, re-ordered Product Backlog that reflects what everyone in the room just learned, ready to feed the next [Sprint Planning](sprint-planning.md).

## Working software over a slide deck

The Guide frames Sprint Review as a working session, and it's explicit that the Scrum Team should avoid narrowing it down to a presentation. The reason is more than a stylistic preference: the entire point of the event is inspecting a real, Done [Increment](increment.md) — one that actually meets the [Definition of Done](definition-of-done.md) — not a description of one. A slide can't be clicked on, can't be interacted with, and can't reveal the gap between "looks finished" and "is finished" the way a stakeholder actually using the feature can. Slides let a team dress up undone work as progress, because a screenshot doesn't crash, doesn't have a broken edge case, and doesn't reveal a missing permission check. Working software does.

This is also why Sprint Review is one of the places the [Definition of Done](definition-of-done.md) does real work: if something demonstrated at Review doesn't actually meet DoD, it isn't part of the Increment yet, no matter how good it looked on screen — it goes back to the Product Backlog as unfinished work, not forward as a shipped result.

## Distinguishing it from "a demo"

A demo is a one-way show-and-tell. Someone drives a screen share, the audience watches, and the event ends when the presenter is done presenting. Sprint Review is supposed to be a working session — stakeholders interact with the product, ask real questions, push back, and redirect priorities in the room, and that conversation is meant to directly change the [Product Backlog](product-backlog.md) before the team plans its next Sprint.

The clearest test of whether a given Sprint Review was a Review or a demo wearing its name: did the Product Owner walk out with the backlog actually re-ordered or refined based on something learned in the room? If the feedback got nodded at, written down "for later," and never touched the backlog, the event functioned as a demo regardless of what it was called on the calendar. Other tells of demo-mode: a fully scripted walkthrough with no room for a stakeholder to say "wait, can I try that" or "why did we build it this way"; stakeholders who show up passively because they've learned nothing they say will change anything; and a Product Owner who treats the meeting as a status update to deliver rather than input to receive.

## Why this matters more than it looks like it should

Sprint Review is one of the highest-leverage points of contact between the Scrum Team and the people who actually decide whether the product succeeds. A team that treats it as a formality is throwing away its best, highest-bandwidth channel for [adaptation](scrum-theory-pillars.md) — a Daily Scrum can only adapt based on what the team itself notices; a Sprint Review can adapt based on what an actual customer-facing stakeholder notices, which the team may never have seen coming. Facilitating a Sprint Review so it stays a genuine working session — rather than sliding back into presentation mode under time pressure — is a core [facilitation](../04-scrum-master-role/facilitation-techniques.md) skill, and it's worth treating as seriously as facilitating the Retrospective.

One more distinction worth holding onto: Sprint Review is never a release gate. An Increment can be delivered to stakeholders before the Sprint even ends, and Sprint Review isn't the moment permission to ship gets granted — see [The Increment](increment.md) for why "Done" and "released" are two separate decisions entirely.
