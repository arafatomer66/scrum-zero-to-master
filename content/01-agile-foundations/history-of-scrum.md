# The History of Scrum

*From a 1986 Harvard Business Review paper about rugby to the 2020 Scrum Guide — the real lineage of the framework, and how the Scrum Master role itself has changed shape along the way.*

Scrum's history matters to a working Scrum Master for a specific, practical reason: the framework has changed meaningfully over four decades, and a lot of what people confidently assert about "what Scrum requires" is actually a leftover from an earlier version that's since been deliberately removed. Knowing the lineage tells you which practices are load-bearing and which are just what your first team happened to do.

## 1986: the rugby metaphor

Scrum's name comes from a Harvard Business Review paper, "The New New Product Development Game," published in January 1986 by Hirotaka Takeuchi and Ikujiro Nonaka. It wasn't about software at all — it studied product development teams at companies like Honda, Canon, Fuji-Xerox, NEC, Epson, Brother, 3M, and Hewlett-Packard, looking at how their fastest, most innovative teams actually worked. Takeuchi and Nonaka found that the best teams didn't work in the traditional relay-race model, where one specialized phase (research, then design, then development, then testing) hands off cleanly to the next, each phase completed before the next begins. Instead, the best teams worked with overlapping phases, cross-functional membership, and a level of team autonomy that let them adapt as they went — closer to a rugby team moving the ball downfield together than a relay team passing a baton at fixed handoff points. That's where the name comes from: "scrum" is the rugby formation where the whole team packs together to advance the ball as a unit.

The paper identified six characteristics shared by these high-performing teams: built-in instability (deliberately ambiguous goals that force the team to figure out its own approach), self-organizing project teams, overlapping development phases, "multilearning" (individuals developing skills outside their narrow specialty), subtle control (management sets boundaries without micromanaging execution), and organizational transfer of learning. None of this was written for software engineers — but the underlying description of how cross-functional, self-organizing teams outperform rigidly sequential ones is precisely the argument the software Agile movement would make fifteen years later, and it's why Takeuchi and Nonaka are correctly credited as intellectual ancestors of Scrum rather than incidental namesakes.

## The mid-1990s: Sutherland and Schwaber formalize it for software

Jeff Sutherland encountered the 1986 paper's ideas and, starting in 1993 at Easel Corporation, applied them to build what's generally considered the first Scrum team in a software context — cross-functional, self-organizing, working in short iterations with regular inspection points, explicitly drawing on the rugby framing. Around the same time, Ken Schwaber was running his own company, Advanced Development Methods, and arriving at a similar model independently through his own frustration with sequential, plan-driven software processes that reliably produced late, over-budget, and misaligned results.

Sutherland and Schwaber met, compared notes, and found they'd converged on largely the same approach from different starting points. In 1995, they jointly presented a paper, "SCRUM Software Development Process," at the OOPSLA conference — the first formal, public presentation of Scrum as a named methodology for software development, with roles, events, and artifacts that are recognizably ancestral to the framework taught today. This 1995 OOPSLA paper is generally treated as the birth of Scrum as an actual software development framework, distinct from the 1986 paper that inspired its name and philosophy.

## 2001: the Agile Manifesto

By the late 1990s, several lightweight methodologies had emerged in parallel — Scrum, Extreme Programming, DSDM, Crystal, Feature-Driven Development, and others — all reacting against the same heavyweight, sequential processes from different angles. In February 2001, seventeen practitioners, including Sutherland and Schwaber, met in Snowbird, Utah, and produced the [Agile Manifesto](agile-manifesto.md): four values and twelve principles that named what all these methods had in common, without merging them into one framework. Scrum didn't become "the" Agile method at this point — it became one of several signatory methods that shared a philosophy, which is an important distinction, because it explains why Scrum, XP, and Kanban remain distinct frameworks today rather than having consolidated into one.

The following year, 2002, Schwaber and Mike Beedle published *Agile Software Development with Scrum*, the first book-length treatment of the framework, which did a lot to popularize Scrum specifically as the framework of choice for teams adopting Agile values — helped by the fact that Scrum's structure (fixed roles, fixed events, fixed artifacts) was easier to adopt wholesale than XP's more technically demanding engineering practices.

## 2009–2020: the Scrum Guide's evolution

For its first years as a popular framework, Scrum didn't have a single canonical definition — different trainers and books described it slightly differently. Sutherland and Schwaber addressed this by publishing the first formal Scrum Guide in 2010, establishing one shared, versioned reference document that both certifying bodies (Scrum.org, founded by Schwaber in 2009, and Scrum Alliance, which Schwaber had earlier co-founded and left) could point back to.

The Scrum Guide has been revised five times since: 2011, 2013, 2016, 2017, and 2020, each pass generally trimming prescriptive detail rather than adding it — a trend consistent with the framework's own stated goal of being a minimal, adaptable set of rules rather than a comprehensive methodology.

The **2020 revision** is the most significant of these and is worth understanding in detail, because it changed several things people still teach as if they were current:

- It removed the three specific questions ("What did I do yesterday, what will I do today, what impediments are in my way") as a prescribed structure for the Daily Scrum, replacing them with the more general requirement that the Developers inspect progress toward the Sprint Goal and adapt the Sprint Backlog accordingly, in whatever format serves that purpose. The three questions still work as a starting technique — the change removed the requirement, not the option.
- It added the **Product Goal** as a new commitment for the Product Backlog, sitting above the Sprint Goal as a longer-term target the Product Backlog serves, giving each of the three artifacts an explicit commitment (Product Backlog → Product Goal, Sprint Backlog → Sprint Goal, Increment → Definition of Done).
- It collapsed the earlier language of "Development Team" as a sub-team inside the Scrum Team into a single, unified **Scrum Team** with three accountabilities — Product Owner, Scrum Master, and Developers — removing the implication that the Scrum Master and Product Owner stand outside the team doing the actual work.
- It shifted the description of the team from "self-organizing" to **self-managing**, a more precise term meaning the team decides who does what, when, and how, without waiting for the earlier language's ambiguity about whether that included choosing what to work on at all.
- It generally shortened and de-jargonized the document's language, consistent with a stated goal of the framework staying minimally sufficient rather than accumulating prescriptive detail over each revision.

## How the Scrum Master role itself has shifted

The formal accountabilities of the Scrum Master haven't changed as dramatically as the surrounding document's tone has. Early treatments of the role, especially as it got adopted at scale through the 2000s and 2010s, frequently drifted in practice toward what's now recognized as an anti-pattern: the Scrum Master as process enforcer or "Scrum police" — someone whose job was mainly to make sure the ceremonies happened on schedule and the artifacts existed in the right format, closer to a project coordinator with a different title than a coach.

Each Scrum Guide revision has pushed language further away from that reading and further toward the Scrum Master as, in the 2020 Guide's own words, a "true leader who serves the Scrum Team and the larger organization." The role's responsibilities were reframed around three areas of service — serving the Scrum Team, serving the Product Owner, and serving the organization — with an explicit expectation of removing organizational impediments, not just team-level ones, and of coaching the organization toward its own adoption of empiricism rather than just running the team's events correctly. This shift tracks closely with how the [career path](../00-start-here/scrum-master-career-path.md) for the role actually plays out in practice: the further a Scrum Master progresses, the more the job looks like organizational coaching and the less it looks like meeting facilitation. For what that service orientation looks like as a practiced skill rather than a Guide statement, see [Servant Leadership](../04-scrum-master-role/servant-leadership.md).

## Why the lineage matters day to day

A Scrum Master who knows this history has a specific practical advantage: when a team member insists "the Daily Scrum has to be the three questions, that's the rule," or a manager insists on a rigid Development Team distinct from the Product Owner because "that's how Scrum works," the accurate answer isn't a vague appeal to being flexible — it's that the Guide itself removed that exact prescription in 2020, and citing the actual revision history is more persuasive than an opinion. The framework's willingness to remove prescriptive detail over time, rather than accumulate it, is also itself evidence of the empiricism it teaches — see [Empiricism and Lean Thinking](empiricism-and-lean-thinking.md) — being applied reflexively to the Guide's own evolution.
